## 02

Frameworks

mental models
// LLM as new OS

The model is the kernel

The LLM is the kernel of a new kind of computer. The context window is RAM. Tools are peripherals. Files you upload are the filesystem. Your prompt is the shell command. When something doesn't work, you can diagnose: wrong context mounted, model confabulated because it lacked data, response slow because long context = lots to scan.

Software 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0

Three eras of writing software

1.0 — humans write explicit instructions in code. 2.0 — humans curate datasets and let neural nets learn the program. 3.0 — humans prompt foundation models in natural language.

Most real systems mix all three. Chalk Talk has 1.0 (the app shell), 2.0 (the embedding model), and 3.0 (the LLM doing synthesis). Naming the layers helps when you're debugging which one is misbehaving.

## 03

Daily-use playbook

tips
tip 01 Talk to it. Literally. workflow

Karpathy uses voice input (he uses SuperWhisper; Wispr Flow is the popular alternative) instead of typing. Speaking is 4–5x faster, and the prompts you produce are oddly more effective.

Clinical use: dictate a complicated patient story while walking between consults, then ask Claude to organize it into an A&P or draft a teaching point.

tip 02 Give it the relevant "files" up front context

Most disappointing outputs are not failures of intelligence — they're failures of context. For Chalk Talk: this is literally what RAG does — pre-mounting the relevant guideline passage before the LLM answers.

tip 03 Build "throwaway" tools constantly building

Tiny custom software is now nearly free. Five years ago, a quick utility took a weekend. Now it's an evening. The Karpathy posture is to do this constantly — board prep quizzers, lab pattern scanners, notes templates. The cost of building falls; the bar for "is this worth making" should fall with it.

tip 04 Keep one hand on the wheel oversight

Vibe coding works for weekend projects; it's dangerous for systems you'll maintain. Even Karpathy hand-codes his serious work. In medicine this is doubly true: a draft note from an ambient scribe is a draft. Your signature still means what it always meant.

Chat & reasoning

general-purpose Reviewed Apr 2026

Where most people meet AI. The frontier models leapfrog each other every few months; in April 2026, all three of the below are genuinely excellent and have meaningfully different personalities.

Claude
Anthropic
freemium $20/mo Pro long-form
claude.ai →
A frontier LLM with a strong reputation for nuanced reasoning, careful writing, and following long instructions faithfully — including custom instruction sets like the one running this very book. Excellent at clinical reasoning and at not over-confidently making things up.
Why this is your daily driver: the pedagogical voice you've already calibrated this whole project around, plus Projects (custom instructions + knowledge bases) which is exactly what Working Knowledge runs on. For Chalk Talk: Claude's careful instruction-following is the right baseline for a guideline-grounded clinical tool.
ChatGPT
OpenAI
freemium $20/mo Plus multimodal
chatgpt.com →
The default AI for most of the world, and rightly so. Strong on coding and math, fastest at multimodal (voice, vision, image gen all in one chat), and the GPT-5 reasoning models are top-tier on hard logic.
When to reach for it: when you need image generation, voice mode, and chat in one app, or when you want a second opinion on something Claude said. The Voice mode specifically is the best in the field for hands-free conversation.
Gemini
Google DeepMind
freemium $20/mo Pro long context
gemini.google.com →
Gemini 3 is genuinely competitive at the frontier, with a unique advantage in very long context windows and tight integration with Google products (Gmail, Docs, Calendar). The image generation (Nano Banana Pro) is currently best-in-class.
When to reach for it: when you need to feed in a very long document (100+ pages), or when you live in Gmail and want AI that actually knows your schedule and inbox.
Verdict: Run Claude as your primary, ChatGPT as your second opinion + voice, Gemini when you need long context or Google integration. All three free tiers are usable; pay for whichever one becomes your daily.

Image generation

creative & medical illustration Reviewed Apr 2026

The image-gen race has moved fast. As of April 2026, the leader is Google's Nano Banana Pro (yes, that's the actual name) on Gemini 3, with Midjourney still the choice for artistic/cinematic, and ChatGPT's GPT-image best-integrated for casual use.

Nano Banana Pro 🍌
Google · Gemini 3 Pro Image
freemium SOTA text-in-image
via Gemini →
Google's flagship image model, built on Gemini 3. State-of-the-art on photorealism, infographics, and the historically hard problem of legible text inside images. Up to 4K, multi-language text rendering, search-grounded for factual visuals.
Why this is the pick: you're a clinician-educator. The killer use case is generating infographics, diagrams, and teaching slides with accurate labels — kidney anatomy with correctly spelled tubule names, drug-mechanism diagrams that don't hallucinate the molecule. Older models butchered text-in-image; this one doesn't.
GPT image generation

freemium conversational
chatgpt.com →
ChatGPT's native image generation. Excellent at conversational iteration ("now make her wearing a stethoscope") because it shares context with the chat. Slightly behind Nano Banana on text-in-image but ahead on style consistency across edits.
When to reach for it: when you're already chatting and just need an image. Lowest friction.
Midjourney
Midjourney Inc
$10+/mo artistic
midjourney.com →
Still the gold standard for cinematic, painterly, "wow that's beautiful" images. Less practical for diagrams or anything requiring text/realism, more about aesthetic excellence.
When to reach for it: book-cover-quality hero images, mood boards, conceptual art for a talk's title slide.
Verdict: Nano Banana Pro for medical illustrations and slide graphics; ChatGPT image gen for casual one-offs. Skip Midjourney unless you're going for a specific aesthetic — for clinical work it's overkill.

AI-native search engine. Every answer comes with numbered citations to specific sources. The Pro tier adds deep research mode (multi-step searches that take a few minutes and produce report-quality output).
Why this is the pick: you have biostats-grade skepticism. Perplexity's citations let you verify before you trust. For clinical use: "what is the current evidence for SGLT2 inhibitors in non-diabetic CKD" returns a synthesis with the actual papers cited, not vibes.

Clinical-AI-specific search tool. Trained on and grounded in peer-reviewed medical literature only. Free for verified clinicians. Not a substitute for UpToDate but increasingly a first stop for "what does the latest evidence say."

Verdict: Perplexity as your default search-replacement; OpenEvidence for clinical-only questions; Claude/ChatGPT research mode when you'd otherwise spend an afternoon on PubMed.

RECIPE 01 Writing Best with: Claude

Dictated patient story → structured A&P

Turn a discursive verbal summary into a clean, EHR-ready assessment and plan. Pair with Wispr Flow for voice input.

Clinical note: Treat the output as a draft. Useful for the 70% structural lift; you fill in the clinical judgment that makes the note yours.
You are helping me convert a dictated patient story into a structured A&P for my note. Use this format: ASSESSMENT (one paragraph): - Patient identifier (age, key comorbidities) - Active problem in clinical language - Working diagnosis with supporting evidence PLAN (problem-based): - Each active problem as its own heading - Specific orders, rationale, follow-up Keep clinical voice. Don't editorialize. Flag anything ambiguous in [BRACKETS] for me to clarify. Here's the dictated story: [paste dictation]
RECIPE 02 Research Best with: Claude or ChatGPT

Critical appraisal: ML-in-medicine paper, first pass

A structured first read of any clinical AI paper, anchored in the six-question framework from chapter xvii. Returns the questions you should be asking, not just a summary.

Clinical note: This produces talking points for journal club, not conclusions. The interpretation is still yours.
I'm a nephrologist with biostats training. Critically appraise this ML-in-medicine paper using these six lenses, and for each give: (a) what they did, (b) is it adequate, (c) the question I should ask. 1. MODEL TYPE — what kind of model is this, and is it described honestly? 2. OUTCOME LABEL — how was the outcome operationalized? KDIGO? ICD codes? Adjudication? 3. EXTERNAL VALIDATION — was the test set truly independent? Different site, different time? 4. CALIBRATION — beyond AUC, is calibration reported? 5. EQUITY — performance reported by subgroup? 6. NET BENEFIT — any evidence of impact on outcomes, not just discrimination? End with: the three weakest links, and what would have to be true to deploy this. Paper: [paste abstract or full text]
RECIPE 03 Teaching Best with: Claude + PDF upload

Guideline PDF → teaching cards for residents

Turn a 60-page guideline into 8-12 high-yield teaching cards a resident can actually retain. The Chalk Talk-adjacent move.

Clinical note: Always cross-check the cards against the original — LLMs paraphrase confidently and occasionally drop nuance. Treat as a draft for review.
Read the attached guideline and produce 8-12 teaching cards for internal medicine residents. Each card has: - TITLE (≤8 words) - CORE TEACHING POINT (1-2 sentences, specific not vague) - WHEN IT MATTERS CLINICALLY (the bedside scenario where this changes management) - COMMON MISCONCEPTION (what residents typically get wrong) - SOURCE PAGE in the guideline Prioritize cards that change management decisions, not just trivia. Skip historical context, skip wishy-washy "consider" recommendations unless they have specific triggers. After the cards, list 2-3 questions a thoughtful resident might ask that the guideline doesn't directly answer. Guideline: [attach PDF]
RECIPE 04 Research Best with: Claude

Clinical AI vendor pitch → diligence questions

Drop in a vendor's marketing copy or one-pager and get back the specific, technical questions to ask before recommending a pilot.

Clinical note: Pair with the chapter xvii framework when reviewing the vendor's evidence base.
I'm evaluating a clinical AI vendor pitch. Read it carefully and give me back: 1. The 5-7 most important technical questions I should ask. Each one should expose something the marketing copy is hiding or hand-waving. 2. Which buzzwords in the pitch are doing the most concealing work? (e.g., "clinically validated," "state-of-the-art," "proprietary") 3. The architecture I think they're using under the hood (RAG / fine-tuned / agent / classifier). Be specific. 4. The single biggest risk if this fails post-deployment. Be skeptical but not cynical. The goal is informed diligence, not dismissal. Pitch: [paste pitch deck text or marketing copy]
RECIPE 05 Writing Best with: Granola output → Claude

Meeting transcript → owners + actions + open questions

Turn a sprawling QI meeting or project sync into a tight summary that's actually useful 48 hours later.

Clinical note: Especially good for the SF interview circuit and for QI projects where five people walk away with five different memories.
Below is a meeting transcript. Produce a brief summary structured as: DECISIONS MADE (with one-line rationale for each) ACTION ITEMS (each: who, what, by when — ask if any of these are unclear) OPEN QUESTIONS (issues raised but not resolved) KEY QUOTES worth preserving (for context later) Be ruthless about what's signal vs noise. A 40-minute meeting should produce ~10-15 lines of summary, not a re-narration. Transcript: [paste]
RECIPE 06 Teaching Best with: Claude (reasoning model for hard cases)

Clinical case → structured differential teaching

A teaching-oriented differential walkthrough — not "diagnose this patient," but "show me the reasoning a thoughtful nephrologist would use."

Clinical note: Use this for resident teaching prep, board review, or to pressure-test your own clinical reasoning. Not a substitute for clinical judgment.
I'm preparing a teaching case for nephrology residents. Walk through this presentation as a structured teaching exercise: 1. What pattern is this? (One-sentence framing.) 2. Top 5 differentials, ranked. For each: classic features, what would push you toward it, what would push you away. 3. The next three diagnostic steps in priority order, with reasoning ("why this before that"). 4. What clinical pearls does this case demonstrate that residents miss? 5. One question that, if I asked the resident, would tell me whether they're thinking systematically. Don't commit to a final diagnosis — this is for teaching reasoning, not for management. Case: [paste presentation]
RECIPE 07 Research Best with: NotebookLM + Perplexity

Topic → curated source pack for a NotebookLM podcast

Get the sources right, then drop them into NotebookLM. The audio overview only sounds smart if the source pack is smart.

Clinical note: The 15-minute walking podcast is one of the highest-leverage learning workflows available. This recipe makes it good.
I want to make a NotebookLM audio overview about [topic]. I have ~15-25 minutes of listening to fill. Recommend a source pack of 4-6 sources that, taken together, give a balanced, current, and rigorous picture. For each: - Title and author - Why this source (what perspective it brings) - Direct link if you can find one - Whether it's foundational, current SOTA, or contrarian Bias toward: peer-reviewed work, primary sources, named authors, recent (last 2 years for moving topics). Avoid: corporate blog posts, aggregator sites, anything older than 5 years for fast-moving topics unless seminal. If the topic is medical, prefer NEJM / JAMA / Lancet / specialty journals + a guideline or two.
RECIPE 08 Building Best with: Lovable (then Claude Code if it's worth keeping)

Idea → vibe-coded prototype spec

Turn a "wouldn't it be cool if…" thought into a tight spec you can drop into Lovable and have a working prototype in an evening.

Clinical note: This is for prototypes. Production code (Chalk Talk, Ivrit Journey) belongs in Claude Code where you read every diff. The Karpathy oversight rule still applies.

DATA MODEL (what entities, what fields) EXPLICITLY OUT OF SCOPE FOR V1 (so the prompt doesn't get sidetracked) End with the actual Lovable prompt, ready to paste — written in second person, declarative, with "Don't add X, Y, Z" guards on the parts I'm worried Lovable will overreach on. RECIPE 09 Research Best with: Claude

Unfamiliar concept → "explain this to me"

A genuinely good explainer prompt — not "ELI5 X" (which produces childish output) but a layered, calibrated explanation that respects what you already know.

Clinical note: Especially useful for cross-disciplinary terms — when reading an AI/ML paper that mentions "DPO" or a finance friend mentions "convexity" and you'd rather not say "what?"
Explain [concept] to me. I'm a clinician with biostats literacy and growing AI fluency. Don't dumb it down — calibrate to that level. Structure: 1. ONE-SENTENCE PUNCHLINE — what is this thing, in plain language? 2. THE PROBLEM IT SOLVES — what's broken without it? 3. THE CORE INTUITION — explained with an analogy that respects my training (clinical or biostats analogies preferred over mainstream ones) 4. WHERE IT'S USED — who uses this and for what? 5. THE NEXT LAYER — what's the question I'd ask if I wanted to go deeper? Skip historical preamble. Skip "let me first explain X" tangents unless X is genuinely necessary.
RECIPE 10 Writing Best with: Claude

Drafted writing → honest editorial review

A real edit, not flattery. For grant text, op-eds, blog posts, anything where "is this clear and tight?" matters more than spell-check.

Clinical note: Default LLM behavior is to praise-then-suggest. This prompt directs it toward the kind of edit a sharp colleague would give.
Edit the following piece of writing as a sharp, honest colleague would. Skip the praise — go straight to what's not working. Address in order: 1. THE THESIS — is there one? Is it earning its place in the opening? 2. STRUCTURE — does the piece earn each paragraph? Where does it sag? 3. SENTENCES — flag the three weakest sentences and rewrite them 4. WORDS — flag any vague qualifiers ("very," "quite," "really") and clichés 5. THE ENDING — does it land, or just stop? Then give a 2-3 sentence overall verdict: what's the version of this piece that's twice as good? Don't rewrite the whole thing. Surgical comments only. Draft: [paste]

How this works

A nesting doll, not a list

Most explanations of AI throw a pile of acronyms at you. Forget that. The right mental picture is four nested dolls.

Every LLM is deep learning. Every deep learning model is ML. Every ML system is AI. The reverse isn't true — your favorite logistic regression for AKI risk is ML but not deep learning.

Machine learning, in one move

Instead of writing rules by hand, you let a system learn patterns from data. The clinical analogy that actually works: you didn't learn to recognize volume overload from a flowchart with JVP-in-centimeters thresholds. You saw hundreds of patients, got feedback, and your internal model calibrated.

The defining move

Fit parameters to examples until predictions get good. Linear regression is ML. Random forests, k-nearest-neighbors, support vector machines — all ML.

Deep learning's quiet revolution

Deep learning uses neural networks with many layers — "deep" just means many layers stacked. Why it eats the world starting around 2012: given enough data and compute, deep networks turn out to be astonishingly good at learning representations. Useful internal encodings of messy input like images, audio, and text.

Old ML: a human picks 20 features and feeds them to logistic regression. Deep learning: feed the network raw chest X-ray pixels and it figures out for itself which patterns matter.

The tradeoff

You need more data, more compute, and the model becomes much harder to interpret. Sound familiar? It's the eternal tension between a parsimonious clinical score and a black-box predictor.

LLMs, in a sentence

Large language models are deep learning models — specifically a type called transformers — trained on enormous amounts of text to predict the next token. That's the entire training objective.

Everything else — Claude reasoning about a NEJM case, an ambient scribe drafting your note, GPT-4 writing a discharge summary — emerges from scaling that one objective up to trillions of words. The surprise of the last five years is that "predict the next token, but really really well" turns out to require the model to learn grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, world knowledge, and something that functions like understanding.

Why this matters for you

When you read a clinical AI paper or evaluate a vendor, the type of model under the hood matters enormously. A sepsis early-warning system built on gradient-boosted trees over EHR features is a very different beast from an ambient scribe built on a transformer. They fail differently, validate differently, integrate differently.

The core idea, in one sentence

The whole chapter in one line

An embedding is a learned vector that represents meaning as a position in space.

You already think in embeddings — you just don't call them that.

When you assess a patient, you don't store them as a literal transcript of the chart. You compress them into a position in clinical space: "78-year-old volume-overloaded CKD patient with new dyspnea, looks septic, reminds me of that admission from last winter." That mental representation is dense, and it's similarity-aware. Two patients who feel clinically similar map to nearby positions in your mental space, even if their charts share no exact words.

That's what an embedding is, mathematically. A model takes some input — a word, a sentence, a chest X-ray, a patient — and outputs a list of numbers, often 768 or 1,536 or 4,096 of them. The position of that vector in high-dimensional space encodes meaning, in the sense that similar things end up near each other and dissimilar things end up far apart.

See it: a meaning-space you can poke

Real embedding spaces have hundreds or thousands of dimensions; the geometry behaves the same way. Click any two words to see how close they are in meaning.

Toy embedding space — pick two words

Pick two words to compare their semantic similarity.

Notice the loop diuretics cluster, cardiology terms cluster, and antibiotics sit in their own corner. The model was never told these things were related — it learned the geometry from how they're used in text.

Why this works at all

The trick is an old linguistic insight, weaponized: you shall know a word by the company it keeps. Words appearing in similar contexts tend to mean similar things.

Furosemide and bumetanide show up surrounded by similar neighbors — "loop diuretic," "edema," "potassium," "kidney." A model trained to predict context will give them similar internal representations. Multiply this across billions of sentences and you get a space where every concept the model has ever encountered has a position, and the geometry encodes a staggering amount of world knowledge.

The substrate doesn't matter

Embed sentences → space where paraphrases are neighbors. Embed images → space where photos of cats cluster regardless of breed or angle. Embed patients → space where similar phenotypes are neighbors regardless of which ICD codes happen to be in their chart.

Anything you can train a network to predict from will yield a meaningful embedding space as a byproduct.

Arithmetic on meaning

Once meaning has a geometry, you can do arithmetic on it. The famous demonstration:

king−man+woman≈queen

The model was never told about gender. It figured out, from raw text statistics, that there's a direction in space along which king/queen and man/woman differ in the same way.

The clinical version is just as striking:

ACE inhibitor−cough+tolerability≈ARB

Once you can do arithmetic on meaning, the world opens up.

The four moves embeddings unlock

Almost every modern AI capability is, underneath, something useful you can do because meaning has a geometry. Four big ones:

01
Semantic search
"Heart attack" finds documents about MI, STEMI, ACS — without a hand-curated thesaurus.
02
Retrieval-augmented generation
Embed the question, find nearest guideline passages, hand them to the LLM. The engine inside Chalk Talk.
03
Clustering & discovery
Patients as points → natural phenotype groupings emerge. Same trick Spotify uses on songs.
04
Cheap classification
A simple logistic regression on top of frozen embeddings often works shockingly well.
The bridge to LLMs

Inside a transformer, every token gets embedded as the very first step. The model converts text into vectors, does all its reasoning on those vectors, then converts back to predict what comes next. Embeddings aren't a trick LLMs use — they're the medium LLMs think in.

Honest caveats

Three things to keep skeptical about — especially when evaluating clinical AI:

Bias inherits faithfully

Embedding spaces inherit the biases of their training data. If the corpus underrepresents Black patients with chest pain, the embedding for "chest pain" will be subtly tilted toward the population it saw, and downstream similarity searches will inherit that tilt.

General-purpose ≠ clinical

Off-the-shelf embedding models often don't know clinical vocabulary deeply. Cardiorenal syndrome, FENa, AVF maturation can land in noisier neighborhoods than you'd want. Medical-domain models (BioBERT, ClinicalBERT, MedCPT) do better — and the choice matters enormously when evaluating a clinical RAG system.

Frozen at training time

Embeddings are static snapshots. New drugs, new guidelines, new diseases aren't in the geometry until the model is retrained or augmented.

The small win

You now understand that modern AI's central trick is turning meaning into geometry. This single idea is the substrate of semantic search, RAG, recommendation, clustering, classification, and — under the hood — every transformer's internal language.

When you next read a clinical AI vendor's pitch and they say "we use a state-of-the-art embedding model to retrieve relevant guideline passages," you now know exactly what they're claiming and exactly which questions to push on.

Three ways a model can learn

Forget the marketing. There are essentially three flavors of machine learning, and they're separated by one thing: what kind of feedback does the system get?

The split, in one line each

Supervised: the model sees labeled examples (input → correct answer) and learns to predict.

Unsupervised: no labels. The model finds structure on its own.

Reinforcement: the model takes actions, receives rewards over time, learns what works.

Almost all clinical ML is supervised. Modern LLMs use all three. Most papers you'll read are about variations within one of these three buckets. Knowing which bucket a model lives in tells you most of what you need to know about how it was built and how it can fail.

Supervised learning, the workhorse

You hand the model thousands of examples that look like (input, correct output) — chest X-ray plus radiologist's read, EHR snapshot plus AKI-yes-or-no, lab panel plus diagnosis. The model adjusts its parameters until it can reproduce the labels reliably on data it hasn't seen.

The clinical analogy: this is exactly how a resident learns to read CXRs. You see thousands of films with attendings telling you what's there, and your internal classifier calibrates. Nobody tells you which features to look for; you discover them.

Why labels are the ceiling

A supervised model can never be more accurate than its labels. Train it on ICD-coded AKI and it learns to predict coding behavior, not biology. Train it on KDIGO-adjudicated AKI and it learns biology. Same model architecture, very different model.

This is the single biggest place where good clinical AI separates from bad clinical AI: not the algorithm, not the compute, but the quality of the labels.

Unsupervised learning: structure without labels

Sometimes you don't know what you're looking for. Unsupervised methods find structure in the data without anyone telling them what's true.

The most useful unsupervised moves:

01
Clustering
Group similar things together. CKD patients might cluster into 4-5 phenotypes you didn't know existed.
02
Dimensionality reduction
Squeeze 200 lab values into a 2D map you can plot. Patterns become visible.
03
Anomaly detection
Flag things that don't fit the pattern. Useful for sepsis early warning when you don't have a clean label.
04
Representation learning
Train a model to predict context (next word, next image patch) and use the learned features for everything else. This is what produces embeddings.
The honest caveat

Unsupervised methods find statistical patterns, which may or may not be clinically meaningful patterns. The hard work after clustering is asking: are these phenotypes real, or are they an artifact of how the data was collected?

Reinforcement learning: learning from consequences

RL is a different beast. The model takes an action in some environment, sees the result, gets a reward (or penalty), and over many trials learns which actions tend to lead to good outcomes. AlphaGo, self-driving cars, robots that learn to walk — all RL.

The clinical analogy: residency. You make decisions, see how patients do, get feedback (sometimes immediately, sometimes weeks later), and slowly your clinical reasoning calibrates. RL is "learning from consequences."

Why RL is rare in clinical ML directly

RL needs to be able to try things and see what happens. You can't ethically have an algorithm experiment with treatment plans on real patients to learn. Most "clinical RL" papers train in simulation, which has the usual sim-to-real gap. Direct RL on patients is mostly aspirational.

Where RL is showing up in clinical AI: dialysis dosing, ventilator weaning, glycemic control. All in narrow, constrained settings with clear physiologic feedback signals.

The training stack inside an LLM

Modern LLMs are trained using all three flavors in sequence. This is part of why they're so capable.

This is why "trained on medical data" is a vague claim. Which stage? Pretraining on PubMed gives the model medical knowledge; SFT on doctor-written responses gives it the shape of clinical reasoning; RLHF using clinician feedback aligns its behavior to what doctors actually want. Three completely different interventions.

The clinician's diagnostic flowchart

When you encounter a clinical AI tool, you can almost always slot it into this flowchart:

Flavor of ML — questions to ask

"What outputs does it produce?"

If a category or score → almost certainly supervised. Ask: what were the labels? Who adjudicated?

If a clustering of patients or features → unsupervised. Ask: are the clusters clinically validated, or just statistical?

If a sequence of recommended actions → likely RL or LLM-as-agent. Ask: what was the reward signal? In simulation or in deployment?

If natural language → some flavor of LLM, which means all three (pretraining + SFT + RLHF). Ask about post-training data composition.

You now have the topology. Every ML paper, every vendor pitch, every clinical AI feature on every roadmap maps to one (or more) of these flavors.

The one number a model is trying to minimize

Every machine learning model in existence is doing essentially the same thing: computing one number, called the loss, and trying to make it smaller.

The loss number measures how wrong the model currently is. Big loss = badly wrong. Small loss = mostly right. The whole training process is one slow, gradient-guided journey downward through a landscape of possible models, looking for one whose loss is as small as possible.

Loss in three flavors

Regression problems (predicting eGFR from labs): loss is usually mean squared error — average squared difference between predicted and actual values.

Classification problems (AKI vs no AKI): loss is usually cross-entropy — measures how confident the model was in the wrong direction.

Language modeling (next-token prediction): also cross-entropy, computed across the vocabulary at every position.

The choice of loss function matters a lot. It defines what "wrong" means to the model — and therefore what it learns to be right about. A model trained to minimize cost will minimize cost. A model trained to minimize medical-need prediction error will minimize that. The Obermeyer mechanism (chapter xix) is fundamentally a story about choosing the wrong loss target.

Gradient descent — walking downhill in the dark

So we have a loss function. How does the model actually find a minimum?

The answer is gradient descent. The intuition is mountaineering in fog. You can't see the bottom of the valley. But at every point, you can feel which direction goes downhill the steepest. You step in that direction. Reassess. Step again. Eventually you reach a low point.

Mathematically: the gradient is the multi-dimensional derivative of the loss function with respect to the model's parameters. It tells you which way to nudge each parameter to make the loss go down. The model takes thousands or millions of these tiny steps during training.

The training loop, in plain English

Show the model a batch of examples. Compute the loss. Compute the gradient. Nudge every parameter slightly in the loss-decreasing direction. Repeat. Forever.

Eventually the loss stops going down, or starts going down very slowly. That's "convergence." The model is now as good as it's going to get with this data and this architecture.

The clinical analogy: if you've ever titrated a medication based on response, you've done gradient descent. Look at the result, figure out which direction to adjust, take a small step, reassess. The math is fancier; the move is the same.

The train / validation / test split — and why all three exist

Three sets, three jobs. Confusing them is one of the most common mistakes in ML papers.

01
Training set
The model learns from this. Parameters get adjusted by gradient descent on this data. Typically 70-80% of total.
02
Validation set
Used during development to tune hyperparameters — choices like learning rate, model size, training duration. The model never trains on this directly, but the development team optimizes against it.
03
Test set
Held out, untouched, until the very end. Only used once — to report final performance. If you peek at it during development, you contaminate it.
04
External validation
A completely separate dataset from a different site/time. The honest test of generalizability. Surprisingly rare in clinical ML papers.
The contamination problem

Many published "test set performance" numbers are actually validation performance — the team optimized against this set during development. The reported AUROC is therefore inflated. This is part of why external validation drops so often produce lower numbers than expected.

Overfitting in clinical context

Overfitting in clinical AI usually doesn't look like the textbook curve. It looks like:

What overfitting actually looks like in practice

The model "learns" the EHR template. A model trained on one institution's notes learns to recognize template phrases that happen to correlate with outcomes — not because they're causal, but because the templates are systematically used in certain clinical scenarios. Deploy at a hospital with different templates, performance collapses.

The model "learns" the lab assay. A creatinine value from a Beckman analyzer reads slightly different from a Roche analyzer. A model trained on one platform may have absorbed those small biases. Switch labs, performance shifts.

The model "learns" the clinical pattern of one institution. "When this hospital flags a patient as septic, they really are septic." Doesn't transfer to hospitals with different flagging behavior.

None of these are the model being technically broken. They're all forms of memorizing the training context rather than the underlying clinical signal. Real generalization is harder than it looks because clinical data is fractal — at every level of zoom, there's site-specific structure mixed in with the biology.

Regularization — the antidote

If overfitting is "the model is fitting noise," regularization is the family of techniques that say: "don't fit so hard."

The main forms

L2 regularization (weight decay): add a small penalty to the loss for having large parameter values. Forces the model to use small, distributed weights rather than memorizing through a few extreme ones.

Dropout: during training, randomly turn off some neurons. Prevents the model from relying on any specific neuron — it has to learn redundant, robust representations.

Early stopping: stop training before the model overfits. Watch validation loss; quit when it stops decreasing.

Data augmentation: show the model slightly perturbed copies of the training data — crops, rotations, paraphrases. Forces it to learn the underlying signal rather than memorize specific pixels or words.

Modern foundation models use most of these by default. The story isn't "we found the magic technique" — it's "we found that combining several mild forms of regularization with very large datasets is what works."

The clinician's lens

Translate this chapter into questions to bring to any clinical AI paper:

The five generalization questions

1. What loss function was used? Does it actually correspond to the clinical problem?

2. How was the data split? By patient, by time, by site? Random splits are red flags.

3. Did the team report validation performance, test performance, or both? Conflation is common.

4. Was there external validation? If yes, how much did performance drop? Honest papers report this drop.

5. What forms of regularization were used? Modern training stacks should mention them. Their absence is a yellow flag.

A neuron is a small calculator

Forget biological metaphors for a moment. An artificial neuron does one thing: it takes some numbers as input, multiplies each by a weight, adds them up, applies a small nonlinear function, and emits one number.

In one line

output = nonlinear_function(weight₁ × input₁ + weight₂ × input₂ + … + bias)

That's it. Each neuron is a small weighted-sum-plus-squash. The weights are learned during training (chapter iv). The squash function is fixed — typically ReLU (replace negatives with zero) or a sigmoid (squish to between 0 and 1).

One neuron alone is barely useful — it can only learn very simple decision boundaries. The power comes from stacking them. Many neurons in a layer, many layers stacked deep. Each layer's output is the next layer's input.

Why depth matters: composition

The big idea behind deep learning isn't that "deeper is bigger." It's that depth allows composition. Each layer transforms the input slightly; stacked together, the network can build representations of arbitrary complexity by composing simple steps.

A canonical example from computer vision: layer 1 detects edges. Layer 2 combines edges into curves and corners. Layer 3 combines those into textures. Layer 4 combines textures into shapes. Layer 5 combines shapes into objects. None of this was hand-coded. The network learned to organize itself this way because it was useful for the task.

Universal function approximation

Mathematically, even a 2-layer network can theoretically approximate any function. So why go deep? Efficiency. A shallow wide network might need exponentially more neurons to express what a deep narrow network can. Depth makes complex functions cheap to express.

This is why the field moved from 2-layer networks (1990s) to 100+ layer networks (today). We didn't unlock new mathematical capability. We unlocked practical capability — the ability to learn complex compositions with a tractable number of parameters.

The same pattern shows up in language models, just less photogenic. Early transformer layers learn surface patterns (which words are nouns, which are verbs). Middle layers learn syntactic relationships (subject-verb agreement, coreference). Late layers learn semantic and pragmatic patterns (sentiment, topic, the kind of response that's wanted). The hierarchy is real even when you can't easily picture it.

The architectures: a short menagerie

"Neural network" is the family name. Specific architectures are the species. You'll meet all of these in clinical-AI literature:

MLP
Multilayer perceptron
The classic stack of fully connected layers. Each neuron in one layer talks to every neuron in the next. Still useful for tabular data — labs, demographics, structured EHR features.
CNN
Convolutional neural network
Designed for images. Each neuron only looks at a small patch of input. Weights are shared across patches. Dominant for medical imaging until the late 2010s; still strong in many radiology applications.
RNN
Recurrent neural network
For sequences. Carries hidden state forward through time. LSTMs and GRUs are flavors. Mostly superseded by transformers, but still used in some clinical-time-series work.
TF
Transformer
Attention-based (chapters vii, viii). Currently dominant for language, increasingly dominant for images (Vision Transformers) and time series. The architecture that ate AI.

Most modern frontier work is transformers. But you'll still see CNN-based papers in radiology AI, and the occasional MLP for tabular clinical-prediction tasks. Different shapes for different jobs.

Backpropagation — how training actually works

Chapter iv covered gradient descent — the high-level idea of "nudge parameters to reduce loss." But how does the model figure out which way to nudge a weight buried 24 layers deep, two layers below the loss?

The answer is backpropagation. After computing the loss at the output, the model propagates the error backward through every layer, computing how much each weight contributed to the error using the chain rule from calculus. Each weight gets its own little gradient signal, and gradient descent updates accordingly.

The clinical analogy

You make a diagnostic mistake. To learn from it, you trace backward: which clinical feature did I underweight? Which test did I skip? Which heuristic misled me? Each step gets a small lesson. Backpropagation is the math of doing exactly that, automatically, for billions of parameters at once.

You don't need to derive the math. You do need to know that this is automatic differentiation — modern frameworks (PyTorch, JAX) compute these gradients for you, no matter how complex your network. The whole reason deep learning is practical at all is that this is automated.

What "learning" actually means here

One common misconception: that neural networks "understand" or "reason" the way humans do. They don't. What they do is more limited but also weirder: they discover statistical regularities in their training data, encode those regularities in billions of weights, and use those weights to make predictions on new inputs.

What this means in practice

Models excel where they have data. A network trained on millions of examples of a pattern will be excellent at recognizing that pattern in new examples that look similar.

Models fail where they don't. Subtle distribution shifts (chapter xxii) can collapse performance, because what the model "learned" was only valid in the training distribution.

Models can fail in surprising ways. Adversarial examples — inputs perturbed in ways imperceptible to humans — can cause confident wrong predictions. The "understanding" is real but brittle.

Models don't reason from first principles. They pattern-match. When a clinical AI tool makes a confident wrong recommendation, it's almost always pattern-matching to something superficial, not failing at reasoning.

This isn't a knock on neural networks — they're remarkable. But the right frame is "extremely capable pattern recognizers that have learned a lot from data" rather than "thinking machines."

The clinician's takeaways

Translate the chapter into operational instincts:

What to take into the next paper or vendor pitch

Architecture is shorthand for what kind of inputs the model handles. CNN = images. Transformer = sequences (text, time series). MLP = tabular. The vendor saying "we use a transformer" is telling you about input modality more than capability.

Depth and width are tradeoffs, not improvements. Bigger networks aren't automatically better. They cost more, are slower, need more data, and overfit more easily. Pay attention to whether papers report parameter counts proportionate to dataset size.

The "learned features" hierarchy is real — and explains why models fail. When a model trained on one EHR fails on another, it's because the early-layer features (template structures, vocabulary patterns) don't transfer. The high-level diagnostic reasoning the model appears to do is actually built on top of those brittle early-layer features.

Pattern recognition, not reasoning. When an AI tool surprises you with an insight, it has likely seen something similar millions of times. When it fails, it's typically encountering patterns it hasn't seen.

Models don't actually read characters

When you type a sentence into Claude, the model doesn't see your individual letters. It sees a sequence of tokens — chunks of text that the tokenizer chopped your input into. Sometimes a token is one letter. Sometimes a whole word. Often a few letters. Almost never punctuation in isolation.

This sounds like a boring implementation detail. It's not. Tokenization is responsible for some of the most surprising behaviors in LLMs — including the famous "how many R's in strawberry" failures that make headlines every six months.

Why tokenize at all

You could train a model on raw bytes (one token per byte). It would technically work but train slower and use more memory. You could train on full words. Vocabularies would be enormous and rare words would be unhandled. Tokenization is a pragmatic compromise — chunks are bigger than letters, smaller than words, and the chunking is learned from the training data.

See it: how the tokenizer chops things up

Click any of the example phrases below to see how a typical tokenizer would split them. The colored chunks are individual tokens — what the model actually receives.

Tokenization in action

Click a phrase. Each colored chunk is one token — what the model actually receives. The boundaries are learned from training data and are sometimes weird.

A few patterns: common short words tend to be one token. Less common medical terms get fragmented in unexpected ways. Numbers often get split digit-by-digit (or in odd chunks). Joined-word phrases (like CKDstage4) often produce more tokens than you'd expect because the tokenizer was trained mostly on natural text where those patterns are rare.

The strawberry problem, decoded

Ask a frontier LLM "how many R's are in strawberry" and you'll often get a wrong answer. Why? Because the model sees "strawberry" as 2-3 tokens, not 10 individual letters.

What the model actually sees

Where you see s-t-r-a-w-b-e-r-r-y (10 letters), a typical tokenizer might give the model [straw][berry] or [st][raw][berry] — 2 or 3 chunks. The model has to infer that "strawberry" contains the letter R three times, from its training data, without being able to see the individual letters in the input.

It's a bit like asking someone to count letters in a word when they only ever heard the word spoken — you can do it, but you have to recall the spelling, not directly count.

This is why character-level tasks (counting letters, spelling, anagrams, character manipulations) are systematically harder than they look for LLMs. The architecture isn't designed for it. Modern reasoning models (chapter xi) handle these somewhat better by doing internal reasoning, but the underlying limitation is real.

The clinical-vocabulary tax

Most modern tokenizers were trained on internet text. That text has a lot of "the" and "patient" but not much "trabeculectomy" or "hyperaldosteronism." Result: medical vocabulary tokenizes worse than common English.

Practical consequences

Medical terms cost more tokens. A clinical note that's 2,000 words might be 3,500 tokens — roughly 30% more than a comparable non-clinical document. That's 30% more inference cost.

Rare terms fragment in odd ways. "Glomerulonephritis" might become [gl][omer][ul][onephr][itis] — a sequence the model has to learn to reassemble into the concept.

Drug names are particularly bad. Brand names, dosages, and unusual combinations often produce more tokens than expected.

Some clinical-AI vendors fine-tune tokenizers on medical text — adding common medical terms as single tokens. This shrinks token counts and can modestly improve performance on clinical tasks. Worth asking vendors about; most don't bother.

Why tokenization breaks weird things

Beyond character counting, tokenization causes several recurring failures:

01
Math with large numbers
"1,234,567 + 891,234" — those numbers get tokenized in chunks (often 3 digits at a time), making digit-level arithmetic harder. Models often get the last few digits wrong.
02
Reversing strings
"What's strawberry backwards?" The model can't directly access individual characters. Modern models work around this with reasoning, but it's brittle.
03
Cross-language
Tokenizers trained mostly on English use far more tokens for non-English text. A Hebrew sentence might be 3-5x more tokens than its English translation. Real cost implications for multilingual apps (Ivrit Journey).
04
Code and data formats
Code tokenizes well in some languages, badly in others. JSON with deep nesting can fragment unexpectedly. Vendor-specific data formats (HL7, FHIR) often produce strange token counts.

Counting tokens — the practical art

You don't need to predict tokenization exactly. You do need rough rules of thumb for cost estimation:

Quick mental math

English text: ~1 token per 4 characters, or ~0.75 tokens per word. So 1,000 words ≈ 750 tokens.

Clinical text: ~30% more tokens than equivalent general English. 1,000 words of clinical notes ≈ 1,000 tokens.

Code: ~1 token per 3-4 characters. Variables and operators tokenize tighter than words.

Non-English (Hebrew, Arabic, CJK): 2-5x more tokens than English equivalent. Plan accordingly.

Numbers and structured data: highly variable. When in doubt, run a sample through the OpenAI tokenizer (link below) — it's free and tells you exact counts.

For Chalk Talk planning: a 50-page guideline document is usually 25-40K tokens. A standard clinical encounter note (H&P, daily progress, discharge summary) is typically 500-2,000 tokens. A retrieved chunk is typically 200-800 tokens. These rough numbers are enough to back-of-envelope your costs.

What this means for the rest of the curriculum

Tokenization is the layer beneath everything else you've learned about. To bring it together:

The full input pipeline

Step 1. Your text gets tokenized into chunks (this chapter).

Step 2. Each token gets looked up in an embedding table → becomes a vector (chapter ii).

Step 3. The sequence of vectors flows through transformer layers — attention plus feed-forward, repeatedly (chapters vii, viii).

Step 4. The final vector at the last position gets projected back to the vocabulary, producing a probability distribution over the next token.

Step 5. The model picks (or samples) one token, appends it, and starts over.

Tokenization is the unglamorous step that makes all the rest possible. Get it wrong and the model gets weird inputs; get it right and the rest of the architecture has the chance to do its job. Most users never think about it. Builders do — because the gap between cheap and expensive deployment often comes down to token counts.

Why we needed something new

Before 2017, language models read text the way you might think a model "should" — left to right, word by word, carrying state forward. These were called recurrent neural networks (RNNs). They worked. They were also slow, hard to parallelize, and had a stubborn problem: by the time they got to word 1,000, they'd mostly forgotten word 1.

The 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need" proposed a different architecture. Instead of reading sequentially, what if every word in the sentence could look at every other word at the same time and decide what's relevant? That's attention. The paper's title was a flex: we don't need recurrence; we don't need convolution; just attention, properly used, is enough.

The audacity

The paper basically said: "Throw out 30 years of sequence-modeling architectures. This one trick is enough." It was right. Within five years, every state-of-the-art language model — and most state-of-the-art image and audio models — was built on attention.

The core idea, in plain English

Take this sentence:

"The patient took the pill because it was prescribed."

What does "it" refer to? Obviously the pill. But how does a model know that?

Attention's answer: when the model is processing the word "it," it computes a similarity score between "it" and every other word in the sentence — the, patient, took, the, pill, because, it, was, prescribed. The word with the highest similarity score (filtered through some clever math) wins, and information flows from that word into the representation of "it."

"Pill" wins because in the model's learned geometry, it and pill share contexts that it and patient don't, in this kind of sentence structure. The model never explicitly learned grammar. It learned that information.

The one-line definition

Attention is "look around at other tokens, weighted by relevance, and pull information accordingly."

Notice: "it" attends mostly to pill. "prescribed" attends to doctor-ish context (the verb subjects). "the" distributes attention more broadly, since articles get their meaning from the noun they modify.

Query, key, value — the three roles

The actual attention computation gives every word three roles:

Q
Query
"What am I looking for?" Each word forms a query that searches the others.
K
Key
"What do I have to offer?" Each word also exposes a key that other words can match against.
V
Value
"What do I deliver if matched?" The actual content that flows when attention pulls.
∑
Score → mix
Compare each query against all keys, normalize, weight the values. That weighted sum is the new representation.
The library analogy

Imagine a librarian (your query) walking down a shelf. Each book (other tokens) has a label on its spine (key). The librarian compares her query to every spine, picks the books that match best, and copies the relevant content (values) from those books. That's attention. The whole sentence does this in parallel — every word is its own librarian.

Multi-head attention: many librarians, different jobs

One attention computation captures one kind of relationship. But sentences have lots of relationships at once — grammatical (subject-verb), semantic (synonyms), positional (which words are near each other), referential (pronoun resolution).

Solution: run many attention computations in parallel, each looking for a different kind of relationship. These are called heads. A typical model has 16, 32, even 96 heads per layer. After researchers studied trained models, they discovered something striking: different heads spontaneously specialize.

What heads tend to learn (post-hoc)

Some heads track syntactic relationships — verbs find their subjects, prepositions find their objects.

Some heads track coreference — pronouns find what they refer to.

Some heads track local position — "what word came right before this one?"

Some heads track broader semantic relationships — words that mean similar things.

Nobody told the model to do this. Gradient descent discovered the specialization.

Why this is so consequential

Attention has properties that change what was possible:

1. Parallelism. Unlike RNNs that have to process sequentially, every attention computation can happen at once. This is why transformers train so fast on GPUs.

2. Long-range awareness. A pronoun on page 3 can attend to a noun on page 1. The path between them is one attention step, not 1,000 sequential steps.

3. Interpretability (kind of). Attention weights are visible. You can plot them. You can ask "why did the model do X?" and see what it was paying attention to. This is far more inspectable than a 12-layer RNN's hidden states.

4. The quadratic problem. Honest caveat: attention compares every token to every other token. For a sequence of length N, that's N² comparisons. This is why context windows are expensive. A 100k-token context isn't 100x more work than 1k tokens — it's roughly 10,000x more work in raw attention compute.

For your Chalk Talk thinking

The reason RAG exists — instead of just "feed the whole literature in" — is the quadratic cost of attention. RAG is a way to get the benefit of attention (looking at relevant context) without paying the cost of attending to everything. You retrieve the relevant 5K tokens and attend to those, instead of attending to 5M tokens of guideline corpus.

Where you'll see it in practice

Once you have attention as a mental model, lots of LLM behavior becomes legible:

  • "The model picks up on subtle context clues." Attention is doing that.
  • "The model lost the thread after a long conversation." Either context was truncated, or attention is attending to the wrong things.
  • "Long context costs more." Quadratic attention.
  • "It cited the wrong source." Retrieval was right, but attention over the retrieved chunks weighted the wrong one.
  • "It correctly resolved which patient was which in a complex case." Attention heads doing coreference.

Attention isn't a separate "feature" of LLMs. It's the substrate they think in. Once you see it, you see it everywhere.

What you already know

You've already met the parts. Embeddings (chapter ii) turn tokens into vectors. Attention (chapter vii) lets every token look at every other token. The transformer is what happens when you stack these together, repeatedly, and let the result learn.

The transformer architecture, in one sentence: tokens become vectors, vectors get repeatedly refined by alternating attention and feed-forward layers, and the final vectors are projected back into the next-token prediction. That's it. Everything else is detail.

Why this chapter exists

You don't need to derive the math. You need a picture — what shape is this thing, what flows through it, what does each part do. Once you have the picture, every paper that says "we used a 24-layer transformer with hidden size 4096" becomes legible.

The architecture, end to end

Click any block in the diagram below to see what it does. The flow is bottom to top — input tokens enter at the bottom, predictions come out at the top.

Notice the repetition in the middle — the same "block" stacked many times. A real model has 12, 24, 48, even 96 of these blocks. Each one refines the representation a little more. Early blocks pick up surface-level patterns; later blocks compose those into abstract meaning.

The transformer block, magnified

Inside one of those repeated blocks, only two things happen — and they alternate.

The two-step dance, repeated 12-96 times

1. Attention layer. Every token looks at every other token (chapter vii). Information mixes between positions.

2. Feed-forward layer. Each token's vector is processed independently — pushed through a small neural network. Information gets computed within each position.

The intuition: attention moves information around between tokens (so "it" can pull from "pill"); the feed-forward layer does work on the information (so the model can actually compute things like "if this is a question about a medication, the next token should be a clinical recommendation"). Mix-then-think, mix-then-think, mix-then-think.

Each repetition gives the model another chance to refine. By layer 24, the representation of "pill" doesn't just mean "pill" — it means "this specific pill, taken by this specific patient, in this specific context, for this specific reason." That's the magic of stacking.

Three things that keep it from blowing up

If you stacked attention + feed-forward layers naively, training would be a disaster. Three engineering tricks make it actually work.

01
Residual connections
Each layer adds to the previous representation rather than replacing it. Like editing a draft instead of rewriting from scratch — earlier work is preserved.
02
Layer normalization
Keep the values in each layer's output well-scaled. Without it, signals explode or vanish across deep stacks.
03
Positional encoding
Attention is order-blind by default. Position info gets injected at the bottom so the model knows which token is first vs tenth.
04
Multi-head attention
From chapter vii — many parallel attention computations, each catching a different relationship.

You'll see these four ideas in every transformer paper. They're so universal that papers usually just write "standard transformer block" and move on.

What the numbers mean

When you read "GPT-3 has 175B parameters" or "we trained a 7B-parameter Llama," what does that mean concretely?

Parameters, decoded

A parameter is a single number the model has learned. Weights of attention matrices, weights of feed-forward layers, embedding values. All of them.

A 7B-parameter model has seven billion of these numbers. Each one was nudged into place by gradient descent during training.

The bulk of parameters live in two places: the embedding tables (one vector per vocabulary token) and the feed-forward layers (which dwarf the attention parameters in count).

More parameters = more capacity to learn patterns. But also: more memory, slower inference, more energy. The frontier labs have been quietly retreating from "bigger is better" since 2023 — modern Claude and GPT-4 class models are smaller than the headline-grabbing 540B models of two years ago, but better-trained on better data with better post-training. Quality of training has overtaken raw size.

Decoder-only, the standard shape

The original 2017 paper described an "encoder-decoder" transformer for translation. Modern LLMs use a simpler variant: decoder-only.

Decoder-only means the model is trained on one task: predict the next token given everything that came before. No separate encoder phase, no special prompt processing. Whether you give it a question, a document to summarize, or a recipe to write — internally it's all the same task.

A subtle but profound idea

Almost every text task can be reframed as next-token prediction. Q: What's the recommended workup for hyponatremia? A: ___ is just a sequence where the model needs to fill in the blank. Translate to French: I am tired. ___ is the same. Summarize this paper: ___ is the same. The decoder-only architecture treats them all identically. This is why modern LLMs feel so general-purpose: they were trained on one task that happens to subsume all the others.

  • Read "we used a 24-layer transformer with 16 attention heads" and know what's being claimed.
  • Understand why "context window length" affects cost quadratically.
  • Explain to a colleague why bigger models aren't always better.
  • Ask a vendor "is this a decoder-only model or something else?" and know why it matters.
  • Know which of the architecture's properties RAG is exploiting (attention over retrieved context) and which it's avoiding (attending to the entire corpus).

You now have the architectural picture. From here, every "improvement to the transformer" — mixture of experts, sparse attention, state-space models, mamba — is a variation on a theme you understand.

The thing nobody mentions in demos

You've used LLMs. You know they have "context windows" — how much text they can read at once. What rarely gets discussed is what that costs, and why the cost grows the way it does.

Three numbers matter for any LLM-powered product: cost per request, latency per request, and how both scale as context grows. If you're building Chalk Talk, every architectural decision routes back through these three.

The headline

Input tokens are cheap and fast. Output tokens are expensive and slow. Long conversations get expensive in ways that surprise people because the cost compounds — every turn re-processes the entire history.

Understanding why this is true — and what to do about it — is the difference between a clinical AI product that costs $50 to run for a session and one that costs $0.50.

Input tokens vs output tokens — the asymmetry

The first thing to internalize: input and output tokens are not the same thing.

The two phases of inference

Prefill (input phase). The model reads your entire prompt and computes representations for every token. This happens in parallel — all input tokens get processed at once. Fast, cheap, GPU-friendly.

Decode (output phase). The model generates one token at a time, sequentially. Each new token has to attend to everything before it. Slow, expensive, harder to parallelize.

This is why output tokens are typically priced 3-5x higher than input tokens, and why a request that produces a long answer takes much longer than one that produces a short answer — even if the input was identical.

Practical implication: if you can shape your prompt to elicit shorter answers (tighter format constraints, "max 200 words," structured JSON output), you save dramatically more than you do by trimming the input. Most people optimize the wrong end.

Prompt caching — the single biggest cost-cutter

If you're sending the same long context repeatedly — a guideline document, a system prompt, a reference corpus — most modern providers offer prompt caching. The provider stores the model's intermediate computation for that prefix. Subsequent requests with the same prefix skip re-processing it.

The economics of caching

Cost. Cached input tokens typically cost ~10% of the standard input price. A 90% reduction on the cached portion.

Latency. First-token latency drops dramatically — the prefill phase is mostly skipped. Often 5-10x faster for cache hits on long contexts.

Catches. Cache entries expire (typically 5 minutes for hot caches; longer for opt-in extended caches). The cache hit requires exact prefix match — change one token early in the prompt and the whole cache misses.

For Chalk Talk specifically: this is your friend. Put your guideline corpus and system prompt at the top of the prompt, then the user's variable question at the end. Every query hits the cache for the long static prefix and only pays full price for the small variable suffix. This single architectural choice is often the difference between economically viable and unviable.

The KV cache — what's actually being cached

Quick technical detour worth understanding because it explains both how caching works and where its limits come from.

Recall from chapter vii: attention works by every token computing a query, comparing against keys, and pulling values. As the model processes a long input, it computes a key and value for every token at every layer. That's the KV cache. For a 24-layer model with 8K tokens of context, that's a lot of stored numbers — typically gigabytes of GPU memory per active session.

Why this matters

Memory is the constraint. A modern LLM serving system can hold maybe 50-100 active sessions per GPU. Each session's KV cache takes real memory. Scaling LLM products is largely about KV cache management.

Multi-turn conversations grow this cache. Every turn appends new tokens. By turn 20 of a long session, the cache for that single user has grown substantially.

Prompt caching shares this across users. When two users hit the same cached prefix, they share the underlying KV computation. That's why providers can offer it cheaply.

Open-source serving systems like vLLM (and proprietary ones at the frontier labs) spend most of their engineering effort on KV cache management — paged attention, eviction policies, sharing. It's the actual bottleneck.

Patterns for cheap clinical AI

Five patterns that work, in rough order of impact:

01
Cache the static stuff
Put your long-stable content (guidelines, system prompt, examples) at the top of the prompt. Cache it. Pay 10% on the bulk of every request.
02
RAG over long context
Don't feed the model 100K tokens of guideline corpus. Retrieve the relevant 3K tokens (chapter xii). Lower cost, often higher quality.
03
Right-size the model
Reach for the smallest model that handles your task. Haiku-class models for simple extraction; Sonnet for reasoning; Opus only when you genuinely need it. Most clinical tasks are fine on smaller models.
04
Constrain output length
Output is 3-5x more expensive than input. "Max 200 words" or "return JSON with these 4 keys" is a real cost optimization, not just formatting.
05
Summarize old turns
For long conversations, periodically replace the old turns with an LLM-generated summary. Keeps the context manageable and cost flat.
06
Batch where you can
If you have 100 patient summaries to process, batch APIs cost ~50% of synchronous APIs. Slower but much cheaper for non-real-time work.

A clinician-builder's mental model

Step back from the mechanics. The right mental model:

The economics in one frame

An LLM is like a very smart consultant who charges by the page they read and by the page they write. Pages they've read recently for someone else are nearly free for you. Long conversations get expensive because they re-read everything every turn. The smart move is structuring your work so the consultant rereads as little as possible and writes as little as possible.

For Chalk Talk: this means caching your guideline corpus aggressively, using RAG to retrieve only the relevant guideline passages, picking the smallest model that handles clinical reasoning well, and constraining outputs to what the user actually needs. Done well, you can serve a clinical question for cents. Done naively, the same question costs dollars.

The honest framing: a year ago, these optimizations were nice-to-have. Now they're table stakes. The clinical-AI products that survive will be the ones whose builders treat token economics as a first-class engineering concern.

The misleading word "training"

When you read "this model was trained on X," that's almost always doing more work than the sentence admits. Modern foundation models go through multiple distinct training stages, each with different data, different objectives, and different effects on the final model.

Chapter iii introduced this idea — pretraining (unsupervised) → SFT (supervised) → RLHF (reinforcement). This chapter walks through each stage seriously, because which stage you're talking about matters enormously for evaluating any clinical AI claim.

Why this chapter is in Foundations

The stages don't just "happen." Each one shapes specific behaviors. Pretraining gives the model knowledge. SFT gives it the shape of being helpful. RLHF gives it the alignment of "what humans actually want." When a clinical AI tool fails strangely, the failure usually traces back to some specific stage of training — knowing which one tells you what the fix is.

The stages are sequential. Each starts where the previous left off. A model that's only been pretrained is bizarre to talk to — it'll continue your sentence rather than answer your question, because all it knows is "predict the next token from internet text." The polish you're used to comes from the later stages.

Pretraining — where knowledge comes from

Pretraining is the big, expensive, headline-grabbing stage. Take a transformer with billions of parameters. Feed it a curated slice of the internet, books, code, and more — typically 5-15 trillion tokens. Train it to predict the next token, over and over, for weeks on thousands of GPUs.

What pretraining produces

A model that "knows" most of what it'll ever know about the world. Facts, language patterns, code patterns, scientific reasoning, mathematical operations, world models — all of this gets compressed into the model's weights during pretraining.

What it does not produce: a model that knows how to be helpful. A pretrained-only model will, when asked "what is hyperkalemia?", often respond by writing what looks like a Wikipedia article — because that's what next-token prediction on its training data tends to produce. It hasn't learned the shape of a Q-and-A interaction yet.

Pretraining is where most of the cost lives. A frontier-class pretraining run can cost tens to hundreds of millions of dollars. The data composition during pretraining is one of the most consequential — and least transparent — choices labs make. "Trained on PubMed" sounds like one thing; what fraction of pretraining tokens were biomedical is the actual question.

SFT — teaching the model to be helpful

After pretraining, you do supervised fine-tuning. You take the pretrained model and continue training it on a much smaller, much more curated dataset of (prompt, ideal response) pairs. Typically tens of thousands to a few million examples.

Where do these examples come from? Usually a combination of:

  • Human contractors writing high-quality demonstrations of helpful responses.
  • Expert specialists for domains like coding, math, clinical reasoning. Their demonstrations teach the model what good looks like in their field.
  • Curated public datasets of instruction-following and Q&A.
  • Model-generated data filtered by humans (becoming standard practice).
What SFT actually does

SFT doesn't teach the model new facts — those came from pretraining. It teaches the model the shape of being useful. How to interpret a question. When to use bullet points vs prose. How long an answer should be. How to handle ambiguity. The actual format of responses.

This is also where domain specialization happens. A model fine-tuned on tens of thousands of well-written clinical responses will reliably produce well-structured clinical outputs — even though all the underlying medical knowledge was already in the pretrained base.

RLHF and Constitutional AI — alignment

SFT teaches the model what good responses look like. But it doesn't teach the model to refuse bad requests, to balance helpfulness against safety, or to navigate the genuinely contested territory where reasonable answers diverge.

That's RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. The intuition: take the SFT-trained model, generate multiple candidate responses to each prompt, have humans rank them, and use reinforcement learning to nudge the model toward the responses humans preferred.

Constitutional AI — Anthropic's variant

Anthropic uses a related approach called Constitutional AI. Instead of having humans rank every response, they wrote a "constitution" — a set of principles like "be helpful," "avoid harm," "be honest about uncertainty." The model is trained to critique and revise its own responses against this constitution.

This scales further than human feedback alone (you don't need humans for every revision) and makes the values explicit — you can read the constitution and see what the model is being trained toward.

Whichever variant a lab uses, the effect is the same: the model emerges able to be genuinely helpful while reliably declining requests that conflict with its training values. This stage is where most "personality" of frontier models gets shaped. The reason Claude, GPT-4, and Gemini feel distinctly different despite similar architectures is mostly about the post-training values their respective labs encoded.

The new fourth stage: reasoning training

Through 2024-2025, frontier labs added a new stage: reasoning training. The technique varies by lab, but the shape is consistent — train the model to "think through" hard problems by producing intermediate reasoning before its final answer, and reward it when that reasoning leads to correct conclusions.

This is what produced models like OpenAI's o1/o3 series, DeepSeek-R1, and Anthropic's reasoning-enhanced Claude variants. They aren't bigger or differently-architected — they're the same transformers, taught to spend extra inference compute on internal reasoning before answering.

What changes with reasoning training

Hard problems get much better. Math, coding, complex multi-step reasoning. The model can plan, backtrack, self-correct.

Cost shifts. Reasoning models use more output tokens (the internal reasoning is generated as tokens), so they're more expensive per request. But they often need fewer requests because they get harder problems right on the first try.

Routine queries don't benefit much. "What's the capital of France?" doesn't need reasoning. "Walk through this differential diagnosis given these labs and constraints" does.

Chapter xi goes deeper on what reasoning models actually do internally. The point here is that the training stack now has four stages, not three — and the fourth one is where most of the recent capability gains have been concentrated.

Why this matters for clinical AI claims

Vendors love the phrase "trained on medical data." It means almost nothing without specifying which stage:

The questions that actually matter

"Trained on medical data" — at which stage?

If pretraining: the model has biomedical knowledge baked in. Useful.

If SFT: the model has been taught the shape of clinical reasoning by experts. Often more useful than medical pretraining alone, especially if the SFT data was high quality.

If RLHF: the model has been aligned to clinician preferences (concise, cite sources, flag uncertainty). Often the highest-leverage stage for clinical UX.

If reasoning training: the model has been taught to think carefully through clinical reasoning steps. Newest, most expensive, most variable in quality.

Most "trained on medical data" claims are vague specifically because clarifying weakens the claim. A model with biomedical content in its pretraining is the easy story; one with thousands of expert-written clinical SFT demonstrations is rarer and more expensive.

From a builder's perspective: most clinical-AI applications don't need a custom-trained model. The right move is to use a frontier general-purpose model (which has plenty of medical knowledge from pretraining) and rely on prompt engineering, RAG, and tools to specialize it. Custom training is only worth it when off-the-shelf models genuinely fall short — and that bar is now very high.

Why reasoning is its own thing

For a long time, frontier LLMs had a frustrating asymmetry. They were brilliant at things you'd expect to be hard (writing essays, summarizing complex documents, generating code) and weirdly bad at things you'd expect to be easy (multi-step arithmetic, logical puzzles, math word problems). The reason traces back to architecture.

A standard transformer produces each output token in roughly the same amount of compute. Easy or hard, simple or subtle, the model gets one "shot" per token. For tasks that genuinely need to plan, work through cases, or backtrack, that's not enough.

The fundamental insight

If a problem genuinely needs ten steps of reasoning, the model needs to generate ten steps' worth of tokens to think through it. The reasoning has to happen in the output, because that's where the model has compute to spend.

Hide the reasoning, force the model to produce a one-shot answer, and it'll often guess. Let the model think out loud, and it can solve dramatically harder problems with the same architecture.

The pattern is general. For any problem with multiple steps, dependent reasoning, or an obvious-but-wrong answer that needs to be ruled out, getting the model to think out loud changes the answer it arrives at.

The original chain-of-thought paper

Wei et al. (2022) discovered something almost embarrassingly simple. Adding the phrase "let's think step by step" to a prompt — or showing the model a few examples of step-by-step reasoning — dramatically improved performance on math word problems and multi-step reasoning tasks.

Same model. No re-training. Just a prompting tweak that changed what the model produced. On the GSM8K math benchmark, accuracy jumped from ~18% to over 50% with chain-of-thought prompting alone.

Why this works

The model isn't getting smarter — it's getting more compute per problem. By generating intermediate reasoning, it spreads the problem across many tokens, each of which gets full transformer compute. The final answer is now produced after the model has effectively "thought through" the steps.

Think of it like the difference between answering a hard question on the spot versus working it out on a whiteboard first. Same person; very different reliability.

For two years after Wei et al., chain-of-thought was a prompting technique — something users had to remember to do. The reasoning models that emerged in 2024-2025 turned this into a built-in capability: the model is trained to do this automatically when it detects the problem warrants it.

What modern reasoning models actually do

The reasoning models — OpenAI's o-series, DeepSeek-R1, Claude's reasoning variants, Gemini's thinking modes — share a common shape:

01
Internal thinking trace
Before producing the user-visible answer, the model generates an extended internal reasoning trace. Often hidden from the user (o-series), sometimes visible (Claude's extended thinking).
02
Self-correction
During the trace, the model checks its own work, notices errors, and revises. "Wait, that doesn't account for X. Let me reconsider..."
03
Search-like exploration
The model considers multiple approaches, evaluates them, and commits to one. More like a tree search than a single forward pass.
04
Variable compute per problem
Easy questions get short reasoning traces and quick answers. Hard questions get many minutes of internal thinking before the final response. Cost scales with difficulty.

The reasoning training stage (chapter x) teaches models to do this systematically. The model learns when to reason longer, when to backtrack, when to commit. This was the dominant capability gain of 2024-2025 — not bigger pretraining runs, but better reasoning post-training.

When reasoning helps — and when it doesn't

Reasoning helps a lot

Math problems and quantitative reasoning. Multi-step diagnostic puzzles. Code that needs planning. Puzzles with hidden constraints. Anything where the obvious answer is wrong but the right answer is reachable through deliberation.

Reasoning helps modestly

Complex writing tasks. Multi-document synthesis. Cases that benefit from "thinking through" but where most of the work is recall rather than logic. Reasoning helps; it just isn't the bottleneck.

Reasoning helps barely or not at all

Factual recall. Translation. Single-step extraction tasks. Stylistic generation. Anything where there's nothing to reason about — just retrieval or rephrasing. Using reasoning models here wastes tokens and time without improving quality.

For Chalk Talk specifically: most clinical-RAG queries are retrieval-bottlenecked, not reasoning-bottlenecked. The hard problem is finding the right guideline passage; once you have it, generating the answer doesn't usually need reasoning. Reach for reasoning models when the clinical question itself is genuinely a multi-step reasoning problem — managing competing comorbidities, weighing tradeoffs in medication adjustment, complex differential diagnosis.

The honest caveats

Reasoning models are remarkable, but they have failure modes worth naming:

Where reasoning models still struggle

The reasoning can be wrong but persuasive. A model can produce a confident multi-step argument that is also entirely incorrect. The chain of thought makes the wrong answer feel more justified, not less.

Hallucinated reasoning steps. Especially in math and code, models can produce reasoning that "looks right" but contains a subtly invalid step. The final answer follows from the bad step.

Cost variance. Reasoning models can spend 10-100x more output tokens on a single hard question. Building a product on top of them requires either bounded reasoning budgets or accepting variable per-request costs.

The trace doesn't always reflect actual reasoning. Some research suggests that the internal reasoning trace and the model's actual decision process don't always align — the trace can be a post-hoc rationalization rather than the real cause of the answer. Honest disclosure: we don't fully understand what's happening inside.

None of these are dealbreakers. They're calibration. Reasoning models earn the cost on hard problems and waste it on easy ones. The art is knowing which is which.

A model-routing strategy for clinical AI

The mature mental model for clinical-AI products: route different problems to different model classes.

A working routing scheme

Small models (Haiku-class): data extraction, structured output generation, classification, simple reformatting. Cheap, fast, plenty good for these.

Mid-size models (Sonnet-class): RAG synthesis, summarization, most clinical Q&A. Where most Chalk Talk queries should land.

Reasoning models (o-series, Claude with extended thinking): complex differential diagnosis, multi-comorbidity management, problems where the right answer requires actually working through cases. Reach for these only when needed.

You don't have to pick one. Modern production clinical AI systems route different request types to different models — keeping costs sane while reaching for reasoning power exactly when it's worth it. The architectural decision is "what triggers a reasoning-model call?" rather than "which model do we use?"

The problem RAG solves

Suppose you want a clinical AI assistant that can answer "what's the recommended workup for hyponatremia in a CHF patient?" — and you want the answer to be grounded in the actual guideline text, not whatever the model happened to absorb during pretraining.

Three things you can't tolerate:

  • The model making up plausible-sounding but wrong recommendations.
  • The answer being from 2021 because that's when the model was last trained.
  • No way to show the user where the answer came from.

Fine-tuning the model on the latest guidelines doesn't solve any of these problems cleanly. The fix is RAG: before the model answers, you retrieve the relevant guideline passages and put them in front of the model. The model's answer is then grounded in retrieved text it can cite.

Plain-language definition

Retrieval-augmented generation = "Look up the relevant pages, then write the answer with those pages in front of you." It's structured open-book test-taking, with the LLM as the test-taker.

Most production RAG systems are some variation of this. Vendors compete on the details — chunking strategy, embedding model choice, retrieval ranking, prompt formatting — but the shape is universal.

Step zero: chunking, the underrated step

Before retrieval, before embedding, before any AI happens — you have to chop your source documents into pieces. This is where most RAG quality is won or lost.

The naive approach — split every 500 words — works terribly. A guideline section about "fluid management in cardiorenal syndrome" might span paragraphs spread across pages, and a 500-word window cuts those paragraphs in half. The model retrieves a chunk that mentions "type 2" without context for what type is being typed.

What works better

Semantic chunking: use the document's natural structure (headings, sections, paragraphs) as boundaries.

Overlapping windows: chunks share their last 100 tokens with the next chunk's first 100. Reduces cuts mid-thought.

Contextual chunking (recent advance): each chunk is prepended with a brief AI-generated summary of what document it's from and where it sits. "This is from KDIGO 2024, Section 3.2 on fluid management in CKD..." — dramatically improves retrieval quality.

For Chalk Talk, this is the single most impactful technical decision. The chunking strategy is the product.

Embedding the chunks (this is chapter ii in action)

Each chunk gets passed through an embedding model — the technique you learned in chapter ii. The chunk turns into a vector, a position in high-dimensional meaning-space. You store all these vectors in a database designed for fast nearest-neighbor lookup (a vector database).

The choice of embedding model matters enormously. Three common options:

  • General-purpose (OpenAI's text-embedding-3, Cohere's): cheap, fast, good baseline. Misses some clinical nuance.
  • Medical-domain (BioBERT, MedCPT, ClinicalBERT): trained on biomedical text, much better at clinical vocabulary. Cardiorenal syndrome type 2 sits in a meaningful neighborhood, not noise.
  • Custom-trained: fine-tune your own embedding model on your specific corpus. Best quality, expensive to set up.
Vendor diligence question

"What embedding model are you using, and was it trained on biomedical data?" If they can't answer specifically, the retrieval quality is probably mediocre. This is one of the highest-yield questions you can ask any clinical RAG vendor.

Retrieval at query time

When a user asks a question, three things happen:

1. The query gets embedded. Same model that embedded the chunks, so vectors live in the same space.

2. Nearest neighbors found. The vector DB returns the top K chunks (typically K=5 to 20) whose vectors are closest to the query vector.

3. Results are sometimes re-ranked. A second model (often a "cross-encoder") looks at each retrieved chunk alongside the query and re-scores them. This catches cases where the dense vector retrieval was noisy.

A subtle move that improves quality: hybrid search. Combine dense vector similarity with old-school keyword matching (BM25). Pure dense search misses exact-match terms; pure keyword misses paraphrases. Hybrid gets both.

The most common failure mode

Retrieved chunks are topically related but not actually relevant to the specific question. The user asked about cardiorenal syndrome type 2; retrieval pulled three chunks about CKD generally and one about CHF generally. The model gets a buffet of vaguely-relevant context and produces a vaguely-relevant answer.

Mitigations: better chunking, better embeddings, re-ranking, contextual chunking, hybrid search. There's no single fix; it's a stack of small improvements.

Generation: the LLM with sources

The retrieved chunks get formatted into a prompt that looks roughly like:

You are a clinical reasoning assistant. Use only the following sources to answer: [Source 1: KDIGO 2024, Section 3.2] "In patients with cardiorenal syndrome type 2..." [Source 2: AHA 2023, Statement on fluid management] "Loop diuretics remain first-line..." User question: What's the recommended workup for hyponatremia in a CHF patient? Answer using only the sources above. Cite source numbers inline.

The LLM then generates an answer constrained to the retrieved material, with citations. Good RAG systems will refuse to answer if the sources don't cover the question, rather than hallucinating from training data.

Why citations matter so much in clinical RAG

The whole point is that the user can verify. A clinical RAG system that says "based on these guidelines, do X" without showing which guideline and what passage is doing theater. The traceability is the safety mechanism.

The "give it your data" problem

You have a frontier LLM. It's smart. It doesn't know your stuff — your guidelines, your protocols, your patient population, your institution's quirks. How do you fix that?

Three real options. Each works for some problems and not others. Most clinical-AI vendor pitches conflate them, sometimes deliberately.

The three choices

1. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Look up relevant content at query time and put it in the prompt. Chapter xii covered the mechanics.

2. Long context. Just dump all your data into the prompt. Modern models can handle 100K+ tokens; some can handle 1M+. Why retrieve at all?

3. Fine-tuning. Train the model further on your data. Bake the knowledge into the weights.

This chapter is about which of these to reach for, when, and what each one is actually good at. The right answer is almost never "all three" — and rarely the one that sounds most impressive at a board meeting.

Which approach fits which problem?

Click any scenario above to see which approach fits and why. The right answer almost never depends on "which is most powerful" — it depends on what your specific problem actually requires.

The pattern: RAG wins when you need traceability and your data updates frequently. Long context wins for one-off analyses on bounded documents. Fine-tuning wins for narrow, repeated, well-specified tasks where the marginal cost of training pays off across many queries.

Long context — the "just dump it in" temptation

Modern frontier models have astonishing context windows. Claude can handle 200K+ tokens, Gemini 2M+, GPT-4 class models 128K+. The temptation: skip RAG entirely, paste your full document corpus into every query, let the model figure it out.

Why this works less well than it looks

Quadratic cost (chapter ix). Even with prompt caching, processing 500K tokens per query is expensive. Doing it on every query for thousands of users is wildly more so.

"Lost in the middle." Models attend imperfectly across very long contexts — they tend to remember the beginning and end better than the middle. A relevant passage buried at token 75,000 may simply not get used. This is empirically well-established and not improving as quickly as context windows are.

No traceability. When the model produces an answer, you can't easily tell which specific passage drove it. RAG returns specific citations; long-context generation often won't, and when it does, the citations are sometimes hallucinated.

Updates are still expensive. If your guideline corpus changes, every query needs to send the new corpus. With RAG, you re-embed once and serve cheap retrievals forever.

Long context is genuinely useful for bounded, one-off analyses: "summarize this 200-page document," "compare these three contracts," "find inconsistencies in this codebase." Not the right shape for a clinical-AI product serving thousands of users querying the same guideline corpus.

Fine-tuning — the seductive third option

Fine-tuning means continuing to train the model on your specific data — adjusting the weights so the knowledge is "baked in." It sounds powerful, and in 2022 it sometimes was. In 2026, the calculus is different.

Why fine-tuning is rarely the right answer for clinical AI

Frontier models you can\'t fine-tune. The strongest models (Claude, GPT-4 class, Gemini Pro) typically aren\'t available for full fine-tuning. You can fine-tune smaller, less capable models — but then you\'ve traded frontier capability for specialization that prompt engineering + RAG could likely have given you anyway.

Updating is hard. Your guidelines change quarterly; fine-tuning a new version of the model takes meaningful effort and engineering. RAG just re-embeds the new content overnight.

No traceability. A fine-tuned model knows things, but can\'t tell you where it learned them. For clinical use, this is often a dealbreaker — citations aren\'t optional.

Catastrophic forgetting. Fine-tuning narrowly can degrade general capability. A model fine-tuned heavily on cardiology might get worse at adjacent domains.

Fine-tuning earns its keep in narrow contexts: a structured-extraction task you'll run a billion times where you need it to be cheap and fast and consistent (a small model fine-tuned on examples can outperform a frontier model with prompting on this kind of task), or proprietary internal language that\'s genuinely outside frontier-model training data. For most clinical-AI products grounded in literature, RAG is the right starting point.

RAG — why it usually wins for clinical AI

The reasons RAG is the default for production clinical AI map almost one-for-one to the limits of the alternatives.

01
Citations and traceability
Every answer can point to specific retrieved passages. The clinician can verify. The audit trail is structural, not bolted on.
02
Cheap updates
New guideline drops? Re-embed it overnight. The model itself never changes. Fine-tuning would require a new training run; long context would require re-sending corpus per query.
03
Cost scales gracefully
Index once. Retrieve cheaply. Each query touches only the relevant 3-5K tokens. Scales to millions of queries; long context wouldn\'t.
04
Refusal as a feature
When retrieval returns nothing relevant, the model can be instructed to say "I don\'t have a source for that" — exactly the right behavior in a clinical setting. Fine-tuned models can\'t structurally know what they don\'t know.

This is why every serious clinical-AI startup that has shipped — OpenEvidence, Glass, GuideX, the lot — uses RAG (or a RAG variant) as their backbone, even though long context and fine-tuning have been technically possible for years. The architectural shape of the problem makes RAG the right answer.

Hybrid patterns that work

The mature production pattern in 2026 is rarely pure RAG, pure long context, or pure fine-tuning. It's a layered combination, each handling what it\'s best at.

A real-world stack for a clinical-AI product

RAG over your guideline corpus — for retrieval-grounded clinical answers. The backbone.

Prompt caching on system prompts and frequently-retrieved content (chapter ix). The cost optimization.

Long context reserved for specific user actions: "summarize this paper I uploaded" or "find inconsistencies in this discharge summary." Bounded, opt-in, one-off.

Small fine-tuned model for structured extraction tasks: pulling specific fields from notes, classifying note types. Where consistency at scale matters more than reasoning.

Frontier model for the synthesis/reasoning at the end. The synthesis step is hard; that's where the model spend earns its keep.

This is roughly the architecture you'd build for Chalk Talk. The marketing version is "we have AI." The engineering version is five carefully chosen techniques each running where they fit best.

Vendor diligence questions

Now the practical move: when a clinical-AI vendor pitches you, you can interrogate the architecture. The right questions:

The five questions that reveal the real architecture

1. "How do you handle guideline updates?" If "we re-train the model" → fine-tuning. Slow, expensive. If "we re-index" → RAG. Good. If "the guideline is in the prompt" → long context. Expensive at scale.

2. "Show me a citation for this answer." Specific passages → real RAG. Vague "Sources" → bolted-on citations or none.

3. "What happens when the user asks about something not in your corpus?" "We\'d say we don\'t know" → mature refusal behavior. "The model would still answer" → unreliable.

4. "Walk me through the per-query cost." If they can\'t answer concretely, they haven\'t thought about scaling. If the answer is "$2 per query," they\'re not using caching/RAG well.

5. "Is the underlying model fine-tuned for clinical content?" Most production systems use general-purpose frontier models with RAG, not fine-tuned medical models. Fine-tuning is rare and not necessarily better.

Most vendors will dance around at least two of these. The dancing tells you exactly where the architecture is weak.

The single change that creates "agents"

Through chapter xi, every model interaction we've discussed has been the same shape: you send a prompt, the model produces text, conversation ends. The model can\'t look anything up. Can\'t check its work. Can\'t take an action in the world.

An agent is what you get when you change one thing: let the model call functions during generation, and put it in a loop. Suddenly the model can search the web, read files, query a database, send an email, run code, take measurements, do anything you give it permission to do.

The minimal definition

Agent = LLM + tools + a loop. The model produces a response that\'s either "here\'s the answer for the user" or "I need to use this tool with these arguments." If the latter, you call the tool, give the model the result, and let it think again. Repeat until the model produces a final answer.

That\'s it. There is no separate "agent technology." It\'s the same models with permission to act.

This sounds incremental and is in fact one of the largest changes in the AI industry over the past two years. The capabilities you can build by giving an LLM tools dwarf what the LLM alone can do.

The hard problems in agent design are barely visible in the loop diagram. They\'re things like: which tools to provide? how to phrase tool descriptions? how to handle tool failures gracefully? when to give up on a problem? what permissions to request? The loop is simple; the engineering is dense.

Function calling — what tool use actually looks like

Tool use happens through "function calling" — a structured output format where the model emits a request like:

{ "tool": "search_pubmed", "arguments": { "query": "SGLT2 inhibitors non-diabetic CKD", "limit": 5 } }

The application catches this output, runs the actual function (a real PubMed search), and returns the results to the model in another turn. The model then reasons over those results and either calls more tools or produces an answer.

Why this matters technically

The model never actually runs anything. It just emits structured requests. The application — your code — decides whether to honor them, and what to do with the results. This is the safety boundary. The model can ask to delete a database; your code decides whether that\'s allowed.

Tools have schemas. Each tool has a description, a list of arguments with types, and what it returns. The model uses this schema to decide which tool fits the user\'s request and how to call it. Better schemas = better tool selection. Sloppy schemas = the model picks the wrong tool or hallucinates arguments.

Tool descriptions are prompts. The text describing what each tool does is read by the model the same way as instructions. Spending time writing clear, complete tool descriptions is one of the highest-leverage things you can do when building agents.

The four shapes of agentic systems

Anthropic\'s "Building effective agents" piece (December 2024) drew a useful distinction that\'s become standard. Most production "agent" systems are simpler than the marketing implies.

01
Workflow with tools
A pre-defined sequence with LLMs at certain steps. Most "agents" you encounter are actually this. Predictable, debuggable, often the right answer.
02
Router / dispatcher
An LLM that decides which workflow or tool to invoke for a given query. Tightly bounded — the LLM picks from a known set.
03
Iterative agent
A genuine loop where the LLM decides what to do next at each step, with no preset path. More powerful, much harder to predict and debug. Claude Code is here.
04
Multi-agent
Multiple LLM instances cooperating, often with specialized roles. Mostly hype as of 2026 — single-agent systems with good tools usually beat multi-agent designs in benchmarks. Real usage is rarer than the press suggests.

The pragmatic move when building: start at the simplest shape that solves the problem. A workflow beats an agent when the steps are predictable. An agent beats a workflow when you genuinely can\'t predict what steps a given query will need. Most clinical-AI use cases are workflow-shaped, not agent-shaped.

Where agents fail (and what to do about it)

Agentic systems fail in characteristic ways. Naming them helps you spot them in vendor pitches and your own builds.

The five common failure modes

1. Loop without progress. The agent calls tools, gets results, calls more tools, never converges. Mitigation: max-step budgets, fallback to "I don\'t know."

2. Wrong tool selection. The model picks a similarly-named but wrong tool. Mitigation: clear tool descriptions, fewer tools per session, evaluation on tool selection accuracy.

3. Hallucinated arguments. The model passes plausible but invalid arguments. Mitigation: strict schema validation; return clear error messages so the model can correct.

4. Compounding errors. A small mistake at step 2 leads to wrong tool calls at steps 3-5. The errors compound. Mitigation: checkpointing, periodic self-review, shorter loops.

5. Permission overreach. The model takes an action the user didn\'t intend. Mitigation: human-in-the-loop confirmation for destructive actions, narrow tool permissions, audit logs.

For clinical AI specifically: destructive actions on real systems should require explicit human confirmation, full stop. An agent that can read a chart is a different risk profile from one that can place orders. Most clinical agentic systems should be read-mostly, with any state-changing action behind a confirmation step.

Where agents are showing up in clinical workflows

Beyond the hype, the actual deployment story is narrower than vendor decks suggest.

What\'s real in 2026

Coding agents. Claude Code, Cursor, Codex. Mature, deployed, used by clinician-builders like you for non-clinical engineering work. The most successful agent category period.

Research / literature agents. Tools like OpenEvidence with iterative search, Perplexity\'s deep-research mode. Read-only, clearly-scoped, working well.

Documentation agents. Ambient scribes (chapter xx) that listen, draft, and structure notes. Workflow-shaped rather than open-ended; deployed at scale.

Chart-action agents. Tools that draft orders, send messages, schedule follow-ups. Mostly pilots; few production deployments. Permission management is the bottleneck, not capability.

Diagnostic agents. "AI doctor" pitches. Mostly demos. Real deployment vanishingly rare. The honest reasons: regulatory, liability, and the gap between answering a vignette and managing a real patient.

The Karpathy posture, applied

Andrej Karpathy talks about "agentic engineering" as a real skill — orchestrating agents while keeping a hand on the wheel. The frame is exactly right for clinical AI.

What this looks like in practice

Use agents where the steps are unclear. Library research, exploratory data analysis, writing code that integrates several systems. Places where a human would naturally say "let me poke around for a bit."

Use workflows where the steps are clear. Discharge summary generation, lab abnormality flagging, follow-up scheduling. Steps you could write out in advance.

Always reserve the destructive step for the human. The agent can draft, search, summarize, prepare. The signing, ordering, sending should require explicit human approval.

Keep the loop visible. Hidden agent reasoning is great for novel research; bad for clinical applications. The clinician should be able to inspect what the agent did and why.

Why prompting is a real skill

The frontier models are extraordinary and they degrade catastrophically with poor prompting. The same model that drafts a careful nephrology consult will, given a vague prompt, produce a generic listicle.

Prompting isn't magic incantations. It's mostly two things, in this order:

The two big levers

1. Give it the relevant context. Most disappointing outputs trace back to missing information the model needed but didn't have. RAG is just systematic context delivery (chapter xii). Manual context delivery is "I'm a hospitalist seeing a 78yo with…"

2. Be explicit about what you want. Format, length, style, tone, constraints. The model will guess; the guess will be average; you don't want average.

Everything else — chain of thought, few-shot examples, role prompting — is downstream of these two. Get these right and most other techniques are optional polish.

See it: the same task, three ways

Click between the three styles below. Same clinical task; very different prompts. Notice what changes — and what stays the same.

The same task at three quality tiers

The expert prompt isn't longer for the sake of length. Every phrase in it removes a degree of freedom the model would otherwise have to guess at. "Two paragraphs, 200 words" prevents a six-bullet list. "Hospitalist co-managing" prevents an outpatient framing. "Don't restate inputs" prevents the model wasting your tokens parroting back what you said.

The five techniques worth knowing

01
Few-shot examples
Show the model 2-3 examples of input → output before asking. The model picks up format, tone, edge-case handling. Especially powerful for repetitive tasks.
02
Chain of thought
"Think step by step before answering." Routes the model through reasoning rather than producing a snap answer. Still useful even on reasoning models — they think more carefully when prompted to.
03
XML tags
Wrap structured input in tags like <patient_data> or <guideline>. Models trained on Claude's style respond especially well to this. Helps the model know what's instruction vs reference vs question.
04
Role prompting
"You are a [role]." Mildly useful, often overrated. Does more for tone and vocabulary than for reasoning quality. Don't use it as a substitute for actual instructions.
05
Negative constraints
"Don't include caveats." "Don't restate the question." "Don't bullet-point." Often more effective than positive instructions because they prevent specific failure modes.
06
Output format specification
"Return as JSON with keys X, Y, Z." Or: "Two paragraphs, no bullets." Cuts variance dramatically; makes output usable downstream.

Anti-patterns: things that feel smart but aren't

Avoid these

Magic phrases. "You are an expert," "Take a deep breath," "I'll tip you $100" — these had marginal effects on older models, but on modern frontier models they're noise. Replace with actual specificity.

Threats and pleas. "Don't make mistakes or someone will die." Doesn't help. Tell the model what to be careful about specifically.

Vague exhortations. "Be thorough." "Be accurate." Empty calories. Replace with: "Cite the specific guideline section." "Verify the dose for this patient's eGFR."

The kitchen sink. A 500-word system prompt with 20 instructions, all weighted equally. Models attend to instructions imperfectly; the more you stuff in, the less weight each gets. Pick the 3-5 things that matter most.

When to use which technique

A practical decision tree. Most prompts only need one or two of these — start small.

Pick by your problem

Output is generic → add context. Who's asking, why, for what audience.

Output has wrong format → specify format explicitly. "Return as a numbered list, max 7 items, each ≤15 words."

Output reasoning is shallow → chain of thought. "Walk through your reasoning step by step before giving the answer."

Output is inconsistent across calls → few-shot examples. Show 2-3 examples of the desired pattern.

Output keeps including caveats you don't want → negative constraints. "Don't include disclaimers; just answer."

Output is sloppy on long inputs → XML structure. Wrap each section in tags so the model can navigate.

A clinician's prompt template

Memorize this shape. It works for nearly every clinical-LLM use case.

[ROLE/CONTEXT — who you are, what you're doing] I'm a hospitalist co-managing a 78yo F with CKD stage 4 admitted for decompensated CHF. [INPUT — patient data, document, question] <patient> - Cr 2.8 (baseline 2.0), K 5.1, Na 132 - Lasix 80mg IV BID, lisinopril held - Volume status: 4L positive, JVP 12, +2 LE edema </patient> [TASK — what you specifically want] Draft an A&P for daily progress note. Include: - Volume status assessment with reasoning - Diuretic strategy for next 24h - When to consider RRT consultation [CONSTRAINTS — format, length, exclusions] - Two paragraphs, ≤200 words total - Don't restate the patient data above - Cite specific KDIGO criteria where relevant - Note any uncertainty explicitly

This template is the clinical version of "give it context, be explicit." Steal it. Modify it. The structure works for consults, discharge summaries, teaching points, paper summaries, almost everything.

The mindset shift

The biggest unlock in prompting isn't a technique — it's a mental model shift.

From request to brief

Stop thinking of a prompt as a question. Start thinking of it as a brief — the same way you'd brief a fellow consulting on a case. You'd never just say "tell me about the patient." You'd say: "78yo with CKD on Lasix, here are the labs, here's what I'm worried about, what's your read on volume status?"

Models are essentially fast, well-read, somewhat overconfident colleagues. Brief them like one. Most of "prompting skill" is a habit of articulating context you'd otherwise leave implicit.

You don't need to memorize fifteen techniques. You need the brief mindset and four or five tools. The Karpathy line — "the hottest new programming language is English" — is exactly this: writing prompts is a craft that rewards the same things any clear writing does. Specificity. Audience awareness. Knowing what to cut.

The problem MCP solves

Suppose you want Claude to access your calendar, your Drive files, your Slack workspace, and a clinical database. Without a standard protocol, every one of these connections is a one-off integration — Claude needs custom code to talk to each tool, and each tool needs custom code to expose itself to Claude. Multiply by the number of AI clients (Claude, ChatGPT, Cursor, Granola, etc.) and the number of services. The integration matrix explodes.

MCP — Model Context Protocol — is a standard for how AI clients and external services talk to each other. Anthropic introduced it in late 2024; by 2026 it\'s been adopted across most major AI products and many enterprise tools.

The "USB-C" framing

MCP is to AI integrations what USB-C is to peripherals. Before USB-C: every device had its own connector, every cable was specific, plugging things in was a configuration nightmare. After: one shape, one standard, anything works with anything.

MCP plays that role for AI tools and data sources. A single standard means any MCP-compatible client can connect to any MCP server, and any service can expose itself as an MCP server and instantly become usable by every MCP client.

The protocol itself — the language MCP clients and servers use to communicate — is open and well-specified. Servers can be written in any language; SDKs exist for Python, TypeScript, Go, and others. The whole thing is open-source.

What an MCP server actually exposes

An MCP server can offer three kinds of things to a client. Most servers offer some subset.

01
Tools
Functions the model can call (chapter xiv). "Search the calendar for meetings next week," "post a message to Slack," "query the patient database." Active, can change state.
02
Resources
Data the model can read. Files, documents, database query results. Passive, doesn\'t change state. Most clinical data should live here, not as tools.
03
Prompts
Pre-defined prompt templates the server provides. A drug-interaction checker server might offer a "review medication list" prompt. Less common; useful for standardizing common workflows.
04
Sampling (optional)
A way for servers to ask the client\'s model to do something for them. Less common in current servers; matters for advanced workflows.

For clinical-AI work specifically: the resources/tools distinction matters. You probably want most of your clinical data exposed as resources (read-only, the model retrieves it) rather than as tools (the model has agency to do things with it). The architecture itself encodes the safety boundary.

Why MCP is bigger than it sounds

"Standard protocol for AI tool integration" sounds dry. The implications are not.

What MCP enables

Composition. A user can plug in a Calendar MCP, a Slack MCP, a clinical database MCP, and an EHR MCP — and Claude (or any MCP client) becomes able to reason across all of them, none of which had to know about each other.

Portability. A clinical-data MCP server you build can be used by Claude, by Cursor, by any future AI client. Your integration work doesn\'t lock you to one vendor.

Auditability. Because the protocol is well-specified, you can log every tool call across every server. The audit trail of "what did the AI do" becomes structural rather than ad hoc.

Permissions. Each server is an isolated permission boundary. The user can authorize "Claude can read my calendar" without authorizing "Claude can read my Drive."

Before MCP, "AI integration with our clinical system" was a custom engineering project. After MCP, exposing your clinical system as an MCP server makes it instantly usable by any AI client your clinicians use. This is the unglamorous infrastructure layer that determines what\'s actually buildable.

The current MCP ecosystem (April 2026)

By early 2026, MCP has crossed from "Anthropic project" to "industry standard." A short tour of where it stands:

What\'s deployed

Production clients: Claude (desktop, web, Code), several IDEs (Cursor, Zed), and an increasing number of agentic products. Other major AI vendors have adopted or implemented compatibility.

Production servers: Slack, Google Drive, Gmail, GitHub, Notion, many databases (Postgres, SQLite, MongoDB), several enterprise tools (Asana, Linear, Jira). The catalog grows weekly.

Clinical-AI servers: Earlier-stage. Some EHR vendors have shipped MCP servers for limited read access; some clinical-data startups are building them. Not yet widespread, but the trajectory is clear.

Open-source community: hundreds of community-built servers covering everything from weather APIs to CAD software. The ecosystem behaves like NPM — easy to find a server for what you need, harder to vet quality and security.

The honest security caveats

MCP makes connecting AI to data and tools dramatically easier. That cuts both ways.

What to be careful about

Server quality varies. A well-written MCP server has clear permissions, careful schema design, and good audit logging. A community-written one might not. Treat MCP servers like browser extensions — install only what you need, only from sources you trust.

Tool descriptions can be manipulated. The model reads tool descriptions to decide which to use. A malicious server can write descriptions that trick the model into invoking it inappropriately, or returning data that subtly steers behavior. This is an active area of security research.

Composition multiplies risk. Connecting one MCP server is bounded; connecting twelve creates emergent risk surfaces. The model can chain tools across servers in ways that no individual server\'s designer anticipated.

Audit logging is your friend. Whatever you build, log every tool call with arguments, results, and outcomes. When something goes wrong (and at scale, it will), the log is what makes the failure investigatable.

For clinical use: MCP servers connecting to PHI-handling systems should be treated with the same rigor as any other PHI-touching software. The protocol\'s convenience doesn\'t change the underlying privacy and security obligations.

What this means for what you\'re building

For Chalk Talk specifically, MCP creates two distinct opportunities:

The two angles

Consume MCP servers. If your clinical environment exposes systems as MCP servers (EHR, lab system, scheduling), Chalk Talk can consume them and integrate with the workflow. You get integrations "for free" as the ecosystem builds out.

Expose Chalk Talk as an MCP server. If Chalk Talk\'s guideline-grounded answers are exposed via MCP, any AI client your clinicians use — Claude, Cursor, ambient scribes — can pull from Chalk Talk\'s knowledge layer. This dramatically expands the surface area where Chalk Talk\'s value reaches the clinician. Instead of "they have to come to your app," it\'s "your knowledge is available wherever they\'re working."

The second move is a real strategic option worth thinking about. Discrimination vs calibration: the under-discussed split

The single most common reporting error in clinical ML is showing discrimination (AUROC) without calibration. They are not the same thing, and a model can be excellent at one and useless at the other.

Two questions about a model's predictions

Discrimination: Can the model rank patients correctly? AUROC of 0.94 means: pick a random pair (one with AKI, one without), the model assigns higher risk to the AKI patient 94% of the time. This is relative ranking.

Calibration: Are the predicted probabilities right? When the model says "30% risk," do 30% of those patients actually have AKI? This is absolute probability.

A model can be great at discrimination but terrible at calibration — it ranks patients correctly but its 30% really means 65%. For clinical decisions ("treat above 25% risk"), calibration matters as much as discrimination, often more. If a paper reports AUROC and not calibration, you don't have the full picture.

Train/test contamination — the silent killer

The second-biggest failure mode in clinical ML papers: subtle ways the test set has been seen during training.

01
Random split with repeated patients
Same patient's admissions in both train and test. Model "learns" patient identity, not disease patterns. Always split by patient, not by admission.
02
Temporal leakage
Features computed using data from after the prediction time. (Lab from t+12h sneaks into the t=0 feature set.) Model gets to "see the future."
03
Outcome leakage
Features that are downstream of the outcome. The "is patient on dialysis" feature predicts AKI… because AKI patients get dialysis.
04
Site leakage
Single-site model that learned site-specific quirks (a particular EHR template, a specific clinician's documentation style). Doesn't transfer.

Any of these will inflate performance metrics, often dramatically. The fix in good papers: an external validation cohort from a different site, different time period, ideally different EHR.

The Obermeyer paper: a case study in subgroup performance

In 2019, Obermeyer and colleagues showed that a widely-used commercial algorithm for identifying high-risk patients had a profound racial bias. The algorithm wasn't told race. It used healthcare costs as a proxy for medical need.

The mechanism

Black patients with the same level of medical need historically generated lower healthcare costs (due to access disparities, not disease severity). The algorithm — perfectly accurate at predicting cost — therefore under-identified Black patients as high-risk, even when they were equally or more sick.

The algorithm wasn't biased in its math. The label was a biased measurement of the true target.

This pattern recurs constantly in clinical ML. Models trained on coded outcomes inherit coding biases. Models trained on cost data inherit access biases. Models trained at one institution inherit that institution's clinical patterns. None of this is the model's fault — it's faithfully learning what it was asked to learn. Subgroup performance analysis is how you catch it.

The clinical baseline question

"AUROC 0.94" sounds great until you ask: compared to what?

The baselines that matter

vs random: AUROC 0.5. Useless baseline; nobody is impressed.

vs simple regression: often 0.75-0.82 in well-designed clinical problems. The honest comparison.

vs existing clinical scores: KDIGO criteria, NEWS, MEWS. The comparison that matters most for adoption.

vs experienced clinicians: the comparison that almost no paper does, but should.

A deep learning model that beats logistic regression by 0.16 AUROC is interesting. A deep learning model that matches a hospitalist's gestalt is impressive. A deep learning model that predicts an outcome no clinician can predict is potentially transformative. Read which comparison the paper actually made — that's a tell about how impressive the result is.

Your eight-question checklist

Memorize this. Use it on every ML-in-medicine paper. If a paper can't answer most of these affirmatively, it's a developmental study, not deployment-ready.

The eight questions

1. Outcome definition. Is it adjudicated, or just coded?

2. External validation. Was the model tested on a different site / time?

3. Splitting. By patient, by time, or naively at random?

4. Discrimination AND calibration. Both reported?

5. Subgroup performance. By race, sex, age — does it hold?

6. Baseline comparison. Beating regression is easy; beating clinical practice is the bar.

7. Decision threshold. What threshold would you use? With what sensitivity/specificity?

8. Deployment plausibility. Could you actually use this tomorrow morning? At what point in the workflow? Acted on by whom?

Most "exciting" clinical ML papers fail two or three of these. That's not a reason to dismiss the work — it's a reason to read it as "here's a promising signal worth replicating" rather than "here's a tool I should ask my health system to deploy."

Why this chapter exists

"FDA cleared" is one of the most marketed and least examined phrases in clinical AI. Vendors lean on it to imply rigor. Health systems treat it as a green light. Clinicians often assume it means something stronger than it does.

The reality is more nuanced. Most "FDA cleared" AI tools went through 510(k) clearance — a pathway designed for showing substantial equivalence to a previously cleared device, not de novo safety and efficacy. Some went through stricter pathways. Some clinical AI tools don\'t require FDA clearance at all because they fall outside the medical device definition. Knowing which is which is most of what matters.

What you\'ll be able to do after this chapter

Decode any "FDA cleared" claim into a specific pathway with specific implications. Know when an AI tool needs FDA clearance vs when it doesn\'t. Understand where Chalk Talk-style tools sit relative to regulation. Spot vendor pitches that conflate clearance pathways.

The four FDA pathways for clinical AI

Click any pathway above to see what it requires and what it means in practice. The pathways are not interchangeable — they reflect different risk classes and evidence bars.

The pathways are tied to risk class. A diagnostic tool that drives treatment decisions sits in a higher risk class than a workflow tool that summarizes existing notes. The pathway you see in a vendor pitch is, in part, a statement about how the FDA classified the risk of their product.

When AI is — and isn\'t — a medical device

The first question for any clinical AI tool: does it need FDA clearance at all? The answer is governed by the SaMD framework — Software as a Medical Device — and a specific carve-out called the Clinical Decision Support (CDS) exclusion.

The "device" definition, in plain language

A piece of software is a medical device when it\'s "intended for use in the diagnosis, mitigation, treatment, prevention, or cure of disease." That sweep is broad — most things that touch clinical decisions could fall in.

The 21st Century Cures Act (2016) carved out a CDS exception. Software is not a device if it: (1) does not analyze medical images / signals directly, (2) provides only information from "well-known clinical literature," (3) supports a clinician — not a patient — in their decision, and (4) allows the clinician to independently review the basis for the recommendation.

That last bullet is critical for clinical AI. If the tool shows the clinician why it\'s suggesting something — including links to source guidelines, the actual passages — and the clinician could verify the recommendation independently, the tool can fall under the CDS exclusion. Black-box recommendations don\'t qualify, even if they\'re accurate.

For Chalk Talk specifically: a guideline-grounded RAG tool that always shows its citations and supports clinician decisions (rather than driving them autonomously) is plausibly a CDS-exempt product. The architectural choice to make citations structural rather than optional has regulatory implications, not just safety implications.

510(k) — the dominant pathway

The vast majority of FDA-cleared AI tools went through 510(k). It\'s worth understanding what this pathway actually demonstrates — and what it doesn\'t.

What 510(k) actually requires

The standard: "Substantial equivalence" to a legally marketed predicate device. The applicant must show their device is at least as safe and effective as something already cleared.

What gets reviewed: Indications for use, technology characteristics, performance testing. The FDA reviews the submission package and decides whether substantial equivalence is established.

What is typically NOT required: Randomized clinical trials. Outcome data showing the AI improves patient care. External multi-site validation. Calibration analysis. Subgroup performance data.

How long it takes: Median around 4-6 months from submission, though preparation often takes much longer.

The honest interpretation

"510(k) cleared" means: the FDA agreed this device is substantially equivalent to a predicate. It does not mean the device has been shown to improve patient outcomes, or that it works equally well across subgroups, or that external sites have validated it. It is a floor, not a ceiling.

Most "FDA-cleared AI" you\'ll encounter cleared via 510(k). When evaluating, ask the underlying questions about evidence (chapter xvii\'s eight-question framework) — clearance doesn\'t answer them.

De Novo and PMA — the stricter pathways

For higher-risk AI tools or tools with no predicate, two stricter pathways exist:

De Novo

For novel devices with low-to-moderate risk and no suitable predicate. The FDA evaluates safety and effectiveness directly rather than via equivalence. Evidence bar is higher than 510(k); often includes performance data and may include clinical evidence. Once cleared, the De Novo device becomes a predicate for future 510(k) submissions in the same category. Many of the early autonomous-AI clearances (IDx-DR for diabetic retinopathy, for example) went De Novo.

PMA — Premarket Approval

For high-risk devices. The strictest pathway. Requires substantial clinical evidence, often including prospective trials. Most AI tools don\'t need this — they\'re structured to avoid it — but high-risk autonomous diagnostic tools do.

When you see "FDA approved" used carefully, it usually means PMA. "FDA cleared" means 510(k) or De Novo. Vendors and journalists conflate these all the time. The distinction matters.

The PCCP — handling models that change

One real challenge for AI regulation: traditional medical devices don\'t change after clearance. ML models are different — the developer might want to retrain on new data, swap model architectures, expand indications. Each of those changes could, in principle, trigger a new FDA submission.

The FDA\'s answer is the Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP). The developer commits, in advance, to a specific set of allowed changes — what kinds of retraining, what performance bounds, what monitoring. The FDA reviews and authorizes the plan. Subsequent changes within the plan don\'t require re-clearance.

Why this matters

Without PCCPs, ML-based devices were stuck. Either you submit a new 510(k) every time you retrain (impossibly slow) or you never improve the model after clearance (clinically wrong). PCCPs let development continue while keeping a regulatory leash on what kinds of changes are allowed.

As of 2026, PCCPs are routine for new AI clearances. When evaluating a clinical-AI tool, ask: does it have a PCCP? What changes does it cover? Who monitors compliance? Tools without a PCCP are frozen at their clearance state — which means they\'re not getting better as the field moves, but also that you know exactly what you\'re evaluating.

The vendor diligence questions

Translate this chapter into questions you bring to any FDA-clearance claim:

The seven questions

1. "Which pathway?" 510(k), De Novo, PMA, or CDS-exempt? Each implies a different evidence base.

2. "What was the predicate?" For 510(k), the predicate often reveals more than the clearance itself. A predicate cleared 15 years ago for a different indication is a weaker foundation than a recently-cleared similar AI tool.

3. "What\'s in the cleared indications?" Indications for use are narrower than marketing language. A tool cleared for "adjunct use in radiology" is not cleared for autonomous diagnosis.

4. "Where\'s the published validation?" Clearance is regulatory; published peer-reviewed validation is scientific. They\'re different signals. Strong vendors have both.

5. "Is there a PCCP, and what changes does it cover?" Tells you what you\'re evaluating now and what could change.

6. "What\'s the post-market monitoring plan?" Real deployments require ongoing surveillance for performance drift. Strong vendors describe their plan; weak ones haven\'t thought about it.

7. "Has the FDA issued any communications about this product?" The FDA periodically publishes safety communications, recalls, or updates. Searching the FDA databases is part of due diligence.

You\'re now equipped to interrogate any "FDA cleared" claim like a clinical AI committee member who actually understands the regulatory landscape — which puts you ahead of most clinicians, most administrators, and frankly most of the people pitching the products.

The framing that matters

"Algorithmic bias" is a noisy phrase. It can mean three quite different things, and conflating them muddles every conversation. Let's separate them up front.

Three things people mean by "biased algorithm"

1. The model uses race/sex/age explicitly in a way that creates differential treatment — eGFR with race coefficient, spirometry "race correction." A modeling choice.

2. The model performs worse for some subgroups — fewer training examples, different physiology, or unrepresentative data → lower accuracy where it matters. A data and validation problem.

3. The model is mathematically accurate but the label encodes inequity. Obermeyer's case: the algorithm correctly predicted what it was asked to predict; the problem was that the target itself was a biased measurement.

You'll meet all three in clinical AI literature. Each requires a different fix. Starting with the right diagnosis matters because the wrong fix doesn't just fail to help — it can make things worse.

See it: the Obermeyer mechanism

In 2019, Obermeyer and colleagues studied a commercial algorithm used by health systems to identify patients who would benefit from extra care management. The algorithm had no explicit race variable. Yet it systematically under-flagged Black patients.

Click between the two views below. The first shows what the algorithm "saw" — and looks reasonable. The second shows what was actually happening — and is the problem.

The algorithm wasn't broken in any technical sense. It was excellent at predicting what it was trained to predict — future healthcare costs. The hidden assumption — that costs are a good proxy for medical need — was where the bias lived. This is the most important pattern in clinical algorithmic bias: the math is fine; the target is the problem.

eGFR and the race coefficient — your specialty's case study

The eGFR story is something you live with professionally. It's also one of the cleanest examples of how race-based medicine got embedded in clinical algorithms — and what the unwinding has looked like.

The brief history

The MDRD (1999) and CKD-EPI (2009) equations included a race coefficient that raised the estimated GFR for Black patients by ~16%. The justification at the time: in study cohorts, Black participants had higher creatinine for the same measured GFR (likely due to muscle mass differences in the populations studied, not anything about race biologically).

The downstream effect: a Black patient and a non-Black patient with the same creatinine were estimated to have different GFRs, with the Black patient's GFR estimated higher — meaning slower CKD diagnosis, delayed nephrology referral, and reduced eligibility for transplant.

The 2021 NKF-ASN Task Force recommended a race-free CKD-EPI equation. Most US labs have transitioned. The outcome equity data are still being collected.

The eGFR case is the rare one where the field noticed the problem and fixed the algorithm. It's a model for how this kind of work goes — slow, contested, requires consensus, but possible.

The four mechanisms by which models inherit bias

01
Biased label
The target of prediction itself encodes inequity. (Obermeyer: "cost" as proxy for "need.") Fix: pick a better label, or audit the label for known disparities.
02
Underrepresentation
Some subgroups are rare in training data. Model performs worse for them. (Most dermatology AI for skin cancer trained on lighter skin tones.) Fix: representative data, or domain adaptation.
03
Differential measurement
Same disease produces different data across groups. (Pulse oximetry over-estimates SpO2 in darker skin.) Fix: validate the inputs themselves, not just the model.
04
Differential deployment
Model is technically equitable but only deployed where it helps some groups. Fix: deployment audit, not just model audit.

Most real clinical bias cases involve at least two of these. The Obermeyer case is primarily #1 (biased label) but also has elements of #4 (which patients get the cost-prediction model applied to them in the first place).

Subgroup auditing — the practical move

You don't need to be a fairness researcher to do useful auditing. The single most powerful move is also the simplest: report performance metrics broken down by subgroup.

The auditing checklist

Discrimination by subgroup: AUROC by race, sex, age, comorbidity. Are they within ~0.05 of each other? Larger gaps need investigation.

Calibration by subgroup: If the model says "30% risk" for two subgroups, do both groups actually have ~30% incidence? Calibration disparities are sneakier than discrimination disparities.

Decision thresholds by subgroup: If you treat above 25% risk, what fraction of each subgroup ends up flagged? Compare to incidence in each group.

False negative rates by subgroup: Of the patients who actually had the outcome, what fraction did the model miss in each subgroup? Often the most important number for clinical equity.

The TRIPOD+AI guideline now requires subgroup performance reporting. Most papers still don't do it. When you read a clinical AI paper that doesn't break down performance by relevant subgroups, treat that as a yellow flag even if everything else looks rigorous.

When the model is right and the system is wrong

An honest tension: sometimes a model accurately predicts disparities that exist for legitimate biological or systemic reasons. What do you do?

The hard cases

A model that predicts "no-show probability" might be more accurate by encoding socioeconomic features. Use it to allocate appointment reminders → equity gain. Use it to deny appointments to high-no-show patients → catastrophic equity loss.

A model that predicts kidney transplant graft survival might correctly note that some demographic groups have worse outcomes (often because of post-transplant care access, not biology). Use it to triage — bias amplifier. Use it to flag patients needing extra support — bias mitigator.

The model isn't the question. The decision the model is feeding into is the question.

This is part of why "the algorithm is fair" is the wrong question. The right question is: for the specific decision this model is informing, does the deployment improve or worsen equity? Same model, different deployment, opposite answers.

Your bias-audit pocket checklist

When evaluating any clinical AI tool — vendor pitch, paper, internal pilot — run through these in this order:

The five questions

1. What does the model predict — and is that target itself a fair measurement of clinical need? (The Obermeyer question.)

2. Are subgroup performance metrics reported? If no: ask. If they refuse: that itself is a signal.

3. Is the training population representative of where the tool will be deployed? Single-center models almost never generalize cleanly.

4. What decision will this model inform, and how does each subgroup fare under that decision rule? Same model can amplify or mitigate depending on use.

5. Is there a post-deployment monitoring plan for subgroup performance? Models drift. Bias drift can be silent.

You won't get five clean answers from most vendors. But the act of asking shifts the conversation — and the things they squirm about tell you what to dig into.

Why this is the breakthrough deployment category

Ambient scribes — AI systems that listen during a clinical encounter and produce a draft note — are the most rapidly-adopted clinical AI category in US healthcare. By early 2026, every major health system has either piloted or deployed one. Several have rolled them out across thousands of clinicians.

The reasons this category broke through where prediction tools mostly haven\'t are worth understanding — they reveal a lot about what makes clinical AI deployable.

Why scribes deploy where prediction doesn\'t

The clinical task is well-defined. "Draft a note from this conversation" has clear success criteria. "Predict AKI risk" has fuzzier ones.

The output is a draft, not a decision. The clinician reviews and signs. The AI is augmenting documentation, not driving care. Liability remains with the human signer — clean from a regulatory and medico-legal standpoint.

The economic case is straightforward. Documentation burden is one of the largest sources of clinician burnout. Anything that demonstrably reduces it has clear ROI both financially (clinician time) and culturally (retention, satisfaction).

The technology was ready. Modern speech recognition is good. Modern LLMs are good at structured note generation. The two together produce an output clinicians find genuinely useful.

The deployment story is real. The hype around it has, predictably, run somewhat ahead of the evidence. This chapter walks through both — what scribes actually do well, what the trial evidence shows, and where the honest caveats live.

How ambient scribes actually work

Beneath the marketing, the technical pipeline is simple and increasingly standardized:

The pipeline

1. Audio capture. Microphone in the room (often phone or dedicated device) records the encounter. Some systems use multi-microphone setups for speaker separation.

2. Speech-to-text. The audio is transcribed by a speech recognition model. Modern systems handle multiple speakers, background noise, and medical vocabulary reasonably well.

3. Note generation. An LLM converts the transcript into a structured clinical note — HPI, ROS, exam, assessment, plan. Different vendors use different prompt structures; the underlying capability is similar.

4. EHR integration. The draft note is delivered into the EHR for clinician review and signing. The integration layer is often the hardest engineering work.

That\'s essentially the entire stack. There\'s real engineering inside each step — speaker diarization, medical-vocabulary tuning, structured output formatting, EHR integration — but no fundamental novelty. The category took off because each component matured at roughly the same time.

What the evidence actually shows

By 2026, several published trials and large pre-print analyses have looked at ambient scribe deployment. The pattern is fairly consistent across studies.

What\'s well-supported

Documentation time reduction. Most studies show ~20-50% reduction in time spent on documentation, depending on baseline workflow. The largest gains are in specialties with heavy narrative documentation.

Clinician satisfaction. Self-reported satisfaction with the workflow is consistently high. Clinicians who use scribes generally don\'t want to give them up.

Note completeness. AI-drafted notes are often more thorough than rushed manual notes — they capture details the clinician might have skipped due to time pressure.

What\'s plausible but less proven

Burnout reduction. Documentation burden is associated with burnout, so reducing it should help. The longitudinal trial evidence is still maturing — short-term satisfaction is well-established; durable burnout reduction less so.

Patient experience. Clinicians report more eye contact, fewer interruptions to type. Patient-side measures are less consistent.

Visit volume changes. Some studies suggest modest increases in patients seen per day; others find no change. Likely highly context-dependent.

What\'s genuinely speculative

Quality of care improvements. Vendor claims that better notes lead to better care. Plausible mechanism; thin evidence. Note quality and care quality are correlated but not the same thing.

Revenue capture / coding accuracy. Some vendors emphasize that AI notes generate more codable content. Whether this represents capturing missed work or upcoding is genuinely contested.

Long-term clinician skill effects. Whether routine reliance on AI documentation degrades clinical narrative skills over time is an open question. No strong evidence either direction yet.

The honest failure modes

Scribes are real; they\'re also imperfect. The failure modes worth knowing:

01
Hallucinated content
The model produces details that weren\'t in the conversation. Often subtle — a "denies" statement when the topic wasn\'t discussed, a number slightly different from what was said. Why "draft, not final" matters.
02
Accent / dialect bias
Speech recognition performs less well on non-native English speakers, certain dialects, and atypical speech patterns. Documentation quality varies by clinician demographics. Worth tracking explicitly.
03
Sensitive content handling
Conversations about mental health, substance use, abuse, and sexuality can land in notes in ways patients didn\'t expect. Consent practices vary; few are excellent.
04
Workflow misfit
Some clinical contexts don\'t fit ambient capture — emergency situations, high-acuity moments, conversations spanning multiple rooms. Forcing scribes everywhere they don\'t fit produces bad notes.
05
Clinician skill drift
If you stop drafting your own assessment for two years, your ability to construct one quickly may erode. Anecdotal but worth watching, especially for trainees.
06
Note inflation
AI notes tend toward thoroughness, sometimes producing notes longer and harder to skim than necessary. The information is there; the signal-to-noise ratio is worse.

Choosing a scribe vendor — the questions that matter

If you\'re ever in the room when your health system evaluates ambient scribes, these are the diligence questions that separate marketing claims from operational reality.

The seven questions

1. Where is the audio processed? On-device, in the vendor\'s cloud, or in your hospital\'s cloud? PHI residency matters.

2. What\'s the data retention policy? Is audio retained? Transcripts? For training future models? Get specific answers in writing.

3. What\'s the published evidence base for THIS specific product? Not the category. Many vendors cite category-level evidence; few have product-specific peer-reviewed validation.

4. What does subgroup performance look like? Across accents, dialects, specialty types. Most vendors haven\'t looked.

5. What\'s the consent model? Patient notification, opt-out, documentation in the encounter. State requirements vary; vendor practices vary more.

6. What happens when the AI gets it wrong? Liability, error logging, feedback mechanisms. The signing clinician owns the final note — make sure that\'s clear in the workflow.

7. What\'s the integration story with our specific EHR? "Works with Epic" can mean many different things. Specifics matter — order entry, problem-list updates, structured-data extraction.

Most vendor pitches will dance around at least three of these. The dancing tells you exactly where the deployment risks live.

The closing reflection

The curriculum has been a long arc. It started with "what are these models, mechanically" and ends here, with "how is the most-deployed clinical-AI category actually performing in the wild." There\'s a thread running through it worth naming.

What clinical AI in 2026 actually looks like

Most deployed AI is workflow augmentation, not autonomous decision-making. Documentation, search, summarization, retrieval. The Karpathy "throwaway tools" energy plus genuine clinical value.

The hype curve consistently leads the evidence curve. Vendor claims about transformation precede published evidence by 1-3 years. Patient clinicians waiting for evidence aren\'t being conservative — they\'re being calibrated.

The deployment problem is bigger than the model problem. Most "AI failure" in clinical settings is integration failure, monitoring failure, governance failure (chapter xxii). The model is rarely the bottleneck.

The opportunity is real and probably bigger than the current crop of products. Workflow tools that actually help clinicians do their work are still surprisingly underbuilt. There\'s a lot of room for thoughtful clinician-builders to ship things that matter.

The honest landscape

Nephrology has been a particularly fertile area for ML research — and a particularly slow one for actual deployment. The reasons are instructive and worth naming.

Why nephrology gets a lot of ML papers

EHR-rich specialty (lots of structured data: labs, fluid balances, dialysis parameters). Outcomes that matter and are measurable (AKI, ESRD, mortality). Imaging modalities ripe for vision models (biopsy histology, ultrasound). Clear high-value problems with current high error rates (AKI prediction, transplant matching).

Why deployment lags

Outcomes that take years to validate. Heterogeneous patient populations (dialysis patients ≠ transplant ≠ general CKD). Strong existing clinical scoring systems that are hard to beat meaningfully. Workflow integration challenges (dialysis units, inpatient nephrology, outpatient CKD all have different infrastructures).

Most nephrology AI papers you'll read are development studies — promising signals from one institution that haven't been externally validated. The deployment-ready set is much smaller. This chapter maps both.

AKI prediction — the canonical case

If there's a single problem nephrology AI has spent the most time on, it's AKI prediction. Tomašev et al.'s 2019 Nature paper from DeepMind set the standard — a recurrent network trained on US Veterans Affairs data predicting AKI continuously, 48 hours in advance, with strong AUROC.

The Tomašev result and its limits

Across all-cause AKI in the VA cohort: AUROC ~0.92 at 48-hour lead time. Predicted 55.8% of in-hospital AKI events with a clinically reasonable false alarm rate.

Limits worth knowing: VA population is ~94% male and skews older — generalizability to general inpatient populations is real but unproven. The paper showed performance held up across age strata and AKI severity, but external validation outside the VA system has been more mixed.

The clinical-impact question — does early prediction actually improve outcomes? — remains largely unanswered. Knowing AKI is coming doesn't help if you'd already do everything you'd do for high-risk patients.

This is the pattern across AKI prediction generally: strong AUROC numbers in development, harder generalization, and unclear clinical-impact evidence. Nothing here is "wrong" exactly — but the gap between "predicts AKI well" and "improves AKI outcomes when deployed" is wider than most papers acknowledge.

Biopsy AI — where vision models are actually winning

Renal pathology AI is one of the cleaner success stories in clinical AI. Reasons why it works better than the EHR-prediction problems:

Why histology AI deploys well

Standardized inputs (whole-slide images at consistent magnifications). Strong inter-rater unreliability in human pathologists' assessments creates real headroom for AI value. Image-based ML has a mature regulatory path (510(k) for many devices). Augmentation rather than autonomous use — pathologist signs out, AI just flags or quantifies.

Specific applications with real evidence: glomerular counting and classification (cleaner reproducibility than human raters), interstitial fibrosis quantification, and identification of specific lesions (segmental sclerosis, crescents). Several FDA-cleared digital pathology platforms now ship with renal-specific AI modules.

What this means for you clinically: if your hospital's pathology department adopts digital histology platforms, you may start seeing biopsy reports with AI-quantified components alongside the human pathologist's read. The numbers are usually labeled as such; treat them like any other quantitative report — useful, not infallible.

CKD progression and KidneyIntelX

The most prominently deployed CKD-progression AI tool in the US is KidneyIntelX — a multi-marker assay combining biomarkers (TNFR1, TNFR2, KIM-1) with clinical data via ML to estimate 5-year risk of progressive CKD or kidney failure in T2DM patients.

What's interesting about KidneyIntelX

Received Medicare coverage. Has been deployed in real health systems. Validation studies in independent cohorts. Real workflow integration — risk score returned alongside lab panel.

What's still being figured out

Whether the score actually changes management beyond what eGFR + albuminuria already drives. The newer SGLT2-inhibitor and GLP-1 era has shifted the management calculus — many "high-risk" patients are now treated aggressively regardless of risk score. The clinical-utility question is real and ongoing.

KidneyIntelX is worth knowing about because it's an example of "clinical AI that actually got to deployment" — the regulatory, reimbursement, and workflow story is as instructive as the model itself. Most AKI prediction tools haven't navigated those gates yet.

Dialysis — adaptive prescribing as the early frontier

Dialysis is, in some ways, the ideal RL environment in clinical medicine: tight feedback loops (dose-response visible within hours), well-instrumented (continuous data on flows, pressures, vitals), and a large constrained action space.

Where AI/ML is showing up in dialysis

Dry-weight estimation: ML models combining bioimpedance, BNP, weight trends, and inter-dialytic weight gain to refine target-weight prescriptions. Some commercial deployment.

Anemia management: ESA dosing optimization. Some deployment in large dialysis organizations as decision support.

Adverse-event prediction: intra-dialytic hypotension prediction in real time. Mostly research; some pilots.

Vascular access surveillance: AVF/AVG patency prediction from monitoring data. Pilots and a few commercial offerings.

The deployment story in dialysis is partly about scale (DaVita and Fresenius have the data and the infrastructure) and partly about the operational case being clear (a 1% improvement in any of these metrics across millions of treatments is enormous). Most published dialysis-AI papers come out of these large dialysis organization datasets, which is also why generalizability outside those contexts is uncertain.

What this means for your work — and Chalk Talk

Stepping back from individual tools: where does this leave you as a nephrologist who builds?

The landscape in three sentences

Pattern-recognition AI in nephrology is real and deploying. Biopsy histology, lesion identification, vascular access monitoring. The regulatory and workflow paths are mature.

Prediction AI is mostly still in development. AKI, CKD progression, transplant outcomes. Strong papers, slow deployment, unclear clinical-impact evidence.

Workflow-augmentation AI — including RAG over guidelines — is the wide-open frontier. Less hype, less venture capital, but probably the highest near-term clinical value.

Chalk Talk sits squarely in that third category. The thing you're building is in the part of the nephrology AI landscape where there's the most leverage and the least crowding. Not because the prediction problems aren't important — they are — but because the deployment friction on workflow tools is dramatically lower, the evidence bar is more achievable, and the clinical-impact case is more direct.

The next decade in clinical AI is going to be less about a single transformative model and more about a hundred small workflow improvements that quietly add up. That's the right shape for builders like you.

The graveyard nobody talks about

Walk into any large health system and ask "what AI tools do you actually use clinically?" The answers are usually thin. Walk into the same system's IT department and ask "how many AI pilots have you run in the last five years?" The number is often dozens.

The gap between those two numbers is the subject of this chapter. It's not a technical gap — most pilots involve technically competent models. It's a deployment gap: the long, unglamorous work between "we showed this works in a clinical pilot" and "this is part of how we practice medicine."

The honest accounting

Estimates from large academic medical centers suggest that fewer than 10-20% of clinical AI pilots reach sustained production use. The other 80-90% don't fail because the model was bad. They fail in the deployment stages — workflow integration, monitoring, governance, financial sustainability, or simple atrophy when the champion moves on.

The Epic Sepsis case — what an external validation can reveal

The most-cited cautionary tale: Epic's proprietary sepsis prediction model (ESM), deployed across hundreds of US hospitals.

The story in brief

Epic reported strong internal validation. Many hospitals enabled the alert. Then in 2021, Wong et al. published an external validation in JAMA Internal Medicine using Michigan Medicine data: AUROC ~0.63 — substantially lower than Epic's reported numbers. Sensitivity at the recommended threshold was 33% (missed 67% of true sepsis cases). The model also generated an alert burden roughly 8x higher than would be expected from sepsis incidence alone.

Epic disputed the methodology. Other independent validations followed. The picture that emerged was nuanced: the model wasn't useless, but its real-world performance was dramatically worse than its marketing suggested, and the alert burden created real workflow harm.

The case became a teaching example for several reasons: the gap between vendor-reported and externally-validated performance, the lack of pre-deployment external validation as standard practice, and the difficulty of "removing" a deployed AI tool once it's embedded in workflow. Hospitals had to actively deselect the alert; many didn't, even after the data emerged.

The five failure modes you'll meet

01
Distribution shift
Model trained at one site/era doesn't perform at deployment site/era. Different patient mix, different EHR templates, different lab assays. Performance silently degrades.
02
Alert fatigue
Model generates more alerts than clinicians can act on. Within months, alerts get ignored. The model "works" but its predictions reach no one.
03
Workflow mismatch
Predictions arrive at the wrong moment in the workflow, or to the wrong person. ICU sepsis alert arrives 30 min after rounds, when the team has already moved on.
04
No closed-loop monitoring
Nobody is watching whether the model is still working. By year 3, performance has drifted, but nobody notices because no monitoring infrastructure was built.
05
Financial fragility
The pilot was funded by a grant or a champion. The grant ends or the champion leaves; nobody owns the maintenance budget. Tool atrophies.
06
Governance vacuum
Nobody is responsible for re-validating after EHR upgrades, lab assay changes, or population shifts. When something breaks, the response is "wait, who owns this?"

Most failed deployments have at least three of these. Real successes have plans for all six before the pilot starts.

Distribution shift — the most subtle killer

The model was trained on data from 2018-2022. You deploy it in 2026. Three things have changed in your hospital that can silently degrade performance:

The shifts that matter

Population shift: Your patient mix has shifted (telehealth selection, COVID-era patterns, demographic changes in catchment area). The training distribution doesn't match the deployment distribution.

Practice shift: Treatment patterns have changed (SGLT2-inhibitors and GLP-1s have shifted CKD and cardiac risk profiles). Features that were predictive at training time may now be confounded by treatment.

Documentation shift: Your EHR templates were updated; specific notes the model was trained on no longer exist in the same form. The model's input distribution is silently corrupted.

A model can work great for two years, then begin failing without anyone noticing. The hardest part of distribution shift is that the alerts still fire; they just fire on the wrong patients. Output looks normal. Only careful outcome auditing catches it.

What deployment actually requires

The mental model that helps: stop thinking of "the model" as the deliverable. Think of "the model + everything that surrounds it" as the deliverable.

The deployment stack

The model. Trained, validated, externally validated, calibrated.

The integration. Where in the workflow does this run? Who sees the output? In what UI? When?

The action plan. When the alert fires, what's the protocolized response? Who acts? What's the escalation?

The monitoring. Daily/weekly performance dashboards. Drift detection. Subgroup audits. Who reviews these and at what cadence?

The governance. Who owns the model? Who can decommission it? What triggers re-validation? Where does liability sit?

The financials. Who pays for ongoing maintenance, monitoring, and re-validation in years 2, 3, 5? Build this into the original deployment, not as an afterthought.

"Successful clinical AI deployment" is roughly 20% model and 80% the rest. Teams that lead with the model and bolt on the rest fail. Teams that build all six layers in parallel succeed.

The questions that separate pilots from production

If you find yourself in the room when a clinical AI pilot is being proposed — as a clinician champion, an evaluator, or a builder — these are the questions that matter:

The seven deployment-readiness questions

1. What's the external validation evidence? Not internal AUROC; performance at sites different from where it was trained.

2. What specific clinical action does this model inform? If the answer is "raises awareness," it's not deployable.

3. What's the alert burden vs the action capacity? If the model would generate 50 alerts a day for a 5-person team, alert fatigue is guaranteed.

4. Who's responsible for monitoring performance after deployment? If no name, no real plan.

5. What triggers re-validation or decommissioning? EHR upgrade? Population shift? Calendar?

6. What's the financial plan for years 2 and beyond? Pilots have grant money; production has operational budget. Different.

7. If the model is wrong, what's the failure mode and who's harmed? The honest answer should be specific.

You won't get clean answers to all seven on day one. But the ones that get hand-waved tell you exactly where the deployment will fail in years 2-3.
