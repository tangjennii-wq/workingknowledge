## 02

Frameworks

mental models
// LLM as new OS

The model is the kernel

The LLM is the kernel of a new kind of computer. The context window is RAM. Tools are peripherals. Files you upload are the filesystem. Your prompt is the shell command. When something doesn't work, you can diagnose: wrong context mounted, model confabulated because it lacked data, response slow because long context = lots to scan.

Software 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0

Three eras of writing software

1.0 — humans write explicit instructions in code. 2.0 — humans curate datasets and let neural nets learn the program. 3.0 — humans prompt foundation models in natural language.

Most real systems mix all three. Chalk Talk has 1.0 (the app shell), 2.0 (the embedding model), and 3.0 (the LLM doing synthesis). Naming the layers helps when you're debugging which one is misbehaving.

## 03

Daily-use playbook

tips
tip 01 Talk to it. Literally. workflow

Karpathy uses voice input (he uses SuperWhisper; Wispr Flow is the popular alternative) instead of typing. Speaking is 4–5x faster, and the prompts you produce are oddly more effective.

Clinical use: dictate a complicated patient story while walking between consults, then ask Claude to organize it into an A&P or draft a teaching point.

tip 02 Give it the relevant "files" up front context

Most disappointing outputs are not failures of intelligence — they're failures of context. For Chalk Talk: this is literally what RAG does — pre-mounting the relevant guideline passage before the LLM answers.

tip 03 Build "throwaway" tools constantly building

Tiny custom software is now nearly free. Five years ago, a quick utility took a weekend. Now it's an evening. The Karpathy posture is to do this constantly — board prep quizzers, lab pattern scanners, notes templates. The cost of building falls; the bar for "is this worth making" should fall with it.

tip 04 Keep one hand on the wheel oversight

Vibe coding works for weekend projects; it's dangerous for systems you'll maintain. Even Karpathy hand-codes his serious work. In medicine this is doubly true: a draft note from an ambient scribe is a draft. Your signature still means what it always meant.

Chat & reasoning

general-purpose

Where most people meet AI. The frontier models leapfrog each other every few months; in April 2026, all three of the below are genuinely excellent and have meaningfully different personalities.

How this works

Each scenario gives you a piece of real-world material — a vendor's marketing copy, a paper abstract, a decision you'd actually face — and asks you to evaluate it using a specific concept from the curriculum. Like a clinical case-based exam, but for AI fluency.

The honest workflow: read the prompt, write your own answer in the box (even one sentence), then reveal the model answer and rubric. Rate yourself honestly — "solid," "partial," or "needs review." Anything you mark needs review is a flag to revisit the relevant chapter.

This is not a test. Your answers don't get graded. The point is the practice of reaching for the concept, then checking your reach. Spaced repetition for AI fluency.

SCENARIO 01 Foundations · ch. i ★★★

A vendor pitches you a "deep learning sepsis predictor"

A startup at the booth says: "Our deep-learning sepsis early-warning system uses 14 EHR variables — vital signs, WBC, lactate, age, comorbidities — fed into a gradient-boosted decision tree, achieving an AUC of 0.89 in our validation cohort."

→ What's wrong with their description? Use the nesting-doll framework.
Model answer

Gradient-boosted trees aren't deep learning. They're a classic ML technique — ensemble of decision trees — that lives at the ML layer of the nesting doll, not the deep-learning layer. The vendor is using "deep learning" as a buzzword.

This isn't necessarily a bad model — gradient boosting on tabular EHR data often outperforms deep nets. But the mislabeling tells you something about the team or the marketing: either they don't know the difference, or they do and they're banking on you not knowing. Both are useful signals.

The right question to ask back: "Just to clarify — is the underlying model gradient-boosted trees, or is there a neural network somewhere?" Their answer will tell you a lot.

You got this if you mentioned:
  • Gradient-boosted trees ≠ deep learning
  • It's still ML, just a different layer of the nesting doll
  • This kind of mislabeling is a red flag (or at least a clarifying question)

SCENARIO 02 Foundations · ch. ii ★★★

Why does "myocardial infarction" return MI papers?

A resident searching your hospital's clinical AI tool types "heart attack management" and gets back papers titled "STEMI door-to-balloon optimization" and "Acute coronary syndrome workup" — none of which contain the words "heart attack."

The resident is impressed and asks how the system knows these are related.

→ Explain in 2-3 sentences using the concept of embeddings.
Model answer

The system isn't doing keyword matching — it's using embeddings to do semantic search. Each phrase ("heart attack," "STEMI," "ACS") gets converted into a vector (a position in high-dimensional meaning-space). Because these terms appear in similar contexts in the medical literature the embedding model was trained on, their vectors end up close together in that space.

When the resident's query becomes a vector, the system retrieves documents whose vectors are nearest. No thesaurus required — the geometry of meaning does the work.

You got this if you mentioned:
  • Words/phrases get turned into vectors (embeddings)
  • Similar meanings → nearby positions in high-dimensional space
  • Search retrieves by distance/similarity, not by string matching

SCENARIO 03 Clinical · ch. ii + bias ★★★

An AI scribe that "doesn't know" cardiorenal syndrome

You're piloting an ambient scribe in your nephrology clinic. After dictating a complex case ("CKD stage 4 patient with new-onset orthopnea, weight gain, refractory edema despite diuretics, BNP 1200, suggestive of cardiorenal syndrome type 2"), you notice the auto-generated summary says: "Patient with kidney disease and possible heart failure."

The vendor says their model is "trained on millions of clinical encounters."

→ Using what you know about embeddings and training data, what's likely going on? What would you ask the vendor?
Model answer

"Cardiorenal syndrome" is a specific concept that lives at a particular position in clinical embedding space — but only if the training data contained that concept enough times for the model to learn its neighborhood. If the scribe was trained mostly on general clinical encounters (primary care, ED), nephrology-specific terms like cardiorenal type 2 may sit in noisy, sparsely-populated regions of the embedding space. The model defaults to a more generic, higher-density region: "kidney disease and possible heart failure."

It's not "wrong" — it's underspecified, because the embedding model doesn't have the resolution to capture nephrology vocabulary the way a domain-trained model would.

Questions to ask the vendor:

  • What embedding model is under the hood? General-purpose (e.g. OpenAI's), or medical-domain (e.g. ClinicalBERT, MedCPT)?
  • What's the specialty composition of your training data? How many nephrology encounters specifically?
  • Have you benchmarked specialty-specific terminology preservation?
  • Can you fine-tune for nephrology, or is it one-size-fits-all?
You got this if you mentioned:
  • Specialty terms may be sparse in training data → noisy embeddings
  • The model "rounds toward" higher-density, more generic concepts
  • Asked about embedding model choice (general vs medical-domain)
  • Asked about training data composition

SCENARIO 04 Applied · Chalk Talk ★★★

Why Chalk Talk uses RAG instead of fine-tuning

A resident interested in building a similar tool asks: "Why don't you just fine-tune an LLM on all the KDIGO and AHA guidelines? Wouldn't that be better than retrieving them every time?"

→ Give them three concrete reasons RAG is the right choice for a guideline-grounded clinical tool.
Model answer

Three reasons:

1. Updateability. Guidelines change. KDIGO updated CKD classification recently; AHA changes hypertension thresholds periodically. With RAG, you swap in the new PDF and you're done. With fine-tuning, you'd need to retrain the model — slow, expensive, and the old guideline ghosts may linger in the weights.

2. Citability. RAG retrieves specific passages you can show the user — "this came from KDIGO 2024, page 47." Fine-tuned knowledge is baked into weights and can't be sourced. For clinical use, citability isn't decorative — it's how you let clinicians verify and how you defend the tool's reasoning.

3. Hallucination control. Fine-tuning teaches the model to imitate the style of guidelines, but the model still generates from its full distribution and can confabulate plausible-sounding non-guideline content. RAG grounds the answer in retrieved text, dramatically reducing hallucination risk.

(Bonus reason: cost. Fine-tuning is expensive; RAG runs on top of any frontier model and benefits when the underlying model improves, for free.)

You got this if you mentioned:
  • Updateability — guidelines change, RAG handles it cleanly
  • Citations / traceability to specific source text
  • Reduced hallucination because answers are grounded in retrieved text
How did you do?
SCENARIO 05 Applied · Karpathy framing ★★★

"It keeps forgetting things mid-conversation"

A colleague tells you: "I've been having a long planning conversation with Claude about a research project. After a while it starts contradicting itself, missing things I told it 30 messages ago, and giving worse answers. Is the model getting tired?"

→ Use the "LLM as new OS" framing to explain what's actually happening — and what they should do.
Model answer

The model isn't getting tired — it has no memory of "tired." What's happening is the context window is filling up. In Karpathy's OS metaphor, the context window is RAM. The earlier you went in the conversation, the further from "current attention" those messages are; eventually they may be summarized, deprioritized, or (if the conversation is very long) effectively pushed out of working attention.

What to do:

  • Start fresh. Open a new conversation, paste in a tight summary of where you were, and continue. You're remounting the relevant "files" with less noise.
  • Use Projects / a knowledge base. Move the durable context (the project description, key decisions) into Project instructions or attached docs, so they're always "mounted."
  • Be explicit about what to keep. "Summarize what we've decided so far" and use the summary as the seed for the next session.

The frame matters: you stop blaming the model for being flaky and start managing context the way you'd manage RAM on a constrained machine.

You got this if you mentioned:
  • Context window = RAM analogy
  • Long conversations exhaust attention/effective context
  • Practical fix: summarize and restart, use Projects, manage context explicitly
How did you do?
SCENARIO 06 Clinical · paper appraisal ★★★

A JAMA paper claims an AKI predictor "outperforms clinicians"

From an abstract: "We developed a deep learning model to predict acute kidney injury within 48 hours using EHR data from a single academic medical center (n = 47,000 admissions, 2018–2022). The model achieved AUC 0.91 on a held-out test set, outperforming nephrologist gestalt (AUC 0.78) on the same cases."

→ As a nephrologist with biostats literacy, list at least 3 critical-appraisal questions you'd ask before believing this result generalizes to your hospital.
Model answer

Strong questions to push on:

  • External validation? Single-center training. Does it work at your hospital? Different EHRs, different patient mix, different documentation patterns. The single-center → external generalization gap kills most clinical AI.
  • Was the test set really held out? Same hospital, same time period, same coding habits. "Held-out" within one center isn't independent in a meaningful sense.
  • What's "AKI"? KDIGO criteria, billing codes, or trained-clinician adjudication? Outcome definition is everything in EHR studies — a model trained to predict ICD-coded AKI is predicting coding behavior, not biology.
  • How was clinician gestalt elicited? Real-time at the bedside, or asked retrospectively from limited chart data? The comparison is unfair if humans had less information than the model did.
  • Calibration, not just discrimination. AUC 0.91 says it ranks patients well. It doesn't say the predicted probabilities are accurate. A miscalibrated model misleads at decision thresholds.
  • Net clinical benefit? AUC isn't outcomes. Does deploying this model actually reduce AKI, or change management in a meaningful way? Most predictors never get this far.
  • Bias and equity audit? Performance by race, by sex, by insurance status. Embedded EHR biases propagate.
  • Workflow and threshold? Even an excellent model is useless if it fires too often (alarm fatigue) or too late (predicts AKI 2 hours before it would have been caught anyway).

The clinical-AI critical-appraisal mantra: internal AUC tells you very little; external validity, calibration, and actual deployed impact tell you everything.

You got this if you mentioned at least 3:
  • External validation / generalizability
  • Outcome definition (KDIGO vs codes)
  • Calibration vs discrimination
  • Fairness of clinician comparison
  • Bias / equity audit
  • Net clinical benefit

SCENARIO 07 Applied · oversight ★★★

"Just vibe-code Chalk Talk v2 in a weekend"

A friend says: "You're spending way too long on Chalk Talk. Just vibe-code v2 in Lovable this weekend — Karpathy says all his code is AI-generated now."

→ Why is this advice partially right and partially dangerous? Use what you know about agentic engineering vs vibe coding.

Partially right: the friend is correct that a quick Lovable build can be a great way to prototype an idea — explore a UI, test a workflow with a resident, see if the new feature is even worth building. SCENARIO 08 Clinical · vendor diligence ★★★

Your CMO forwards a clinical-AI vendor pitch

From the deck: "Our AI platform uses retrieval-augmented generation grounded in the latest clinical guidelines, powered by a state-of-the-art language model and proprietary medical embeddings. The system delivers clinically validated, evidence-based recommendations with full traceability."

The CMO asks for your take in two days.

→ List the top 5 questions you'd put to the vendor before recommending a pilot. (Bonus: which buzzword in the pitch is doing the most concealing?)
Model answer

Five high-yield questions:

  • Which embedding model, and which corpus was it trained on? "Proprietary medical embeddings" tells you nothing. Is it BioBERT? MedCPT? Something they fine-tuned themselves? On what data? This is the single biggest determinant of retrieval quality for clinical content.
  • Which guidelines, and how often updated? "The latest clinical guidelines" is hand-wave. Show me the source list. Show me the last update date. What's the process when KDIGO releases an update — same day, same week, same year?
  • What evals? "Clinically validated" by whom? Where's the eval set, what's on it, what was the methodology, what were the failure modes? An honest vendor has a published or shareable eval; a vague one won't.
  • What does "full traceability" actually look like? Does each recommendation cite the specific guideline passage it came from, with text and page reference? Or does it just append a "Sources" list at the bottom that may or may not match? The first is real RAG; the second is theater.
  • Calibration and refusal behavior. What does the system do when the question falls outside the guideline corpus? Does it confidently make something up, or does it say "I don't have a guideline-grounded answer for this"? Production clinical AI must refuse gracefully.

Bonus — most concealing buzzword: probably "clinically validated." It can mean anything from "we ran it past two doctors" to "we did a peer-reviewed prospective deployment study." Always ask what it means, specifically. Runner-up: "state-of-the-art" — relative to what benchmark, on what date.

You got this if you asked about:
  • Specific embedding model & training data
  • Specific guideline sources and update cadence
  • Eval methodology and failure-mode reporting
  • What citations actually look like in practice
  • Refusal / out-of-scope behavior
  • (Bonus) called out "clinically validated" or "state-of-the-art" as load-bearing buzzwords
