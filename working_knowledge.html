## 02

Frameworks

mental models
// LLM as new OS

The model is the kernel

The LLM is the kernel of a new kind of computer. The context window is RAM. Tools are peripherals. Files you upload are the filesystem. Your prompt is the shell command. When something doesn't work, you can diagnose: wrong context mounted, model confabulated because it lacked data, response slow because long context = lots to scan.

Software 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0

Three eras of writing software

1.0 — humans write explicit instructions in code. 2.0 — humans curate datasets and let neural nets learn the program. 3.0 — humans prompt foundation models in natural language.

Most real systems mix all three. Chalk Talk has 1.0 (the app shell), 2.0 (the embedding model), and 3.0 (the LLM doing synthesis). Naming the layers helps when you're debugging which one is misbehaving.

## 03

Daily-use playbook

tips
tip 01 Talk to it. Literally. workflow

Karpathy uses voice input (he uses SuperWhisper; Wispr Flow is the popular alternative) instead of typing. Speaking is 4–5x faster, and the prompts you produce are oddly more effective.

Clinical use: dictate a complicated patient story while walking between consults, then ask Claude to organize it into an A&P or draft a teaching point.

tip 02 Give it the relevant "files" up front context

Most disappointing outputs are not failures of intelligence — they're failures of context. For Chalk Talk: this is literally what RAG does — pre-mounting the relevant guideline passage before the LLM answers.

tip 03 Build "throwaway" tools constantly building

Tiny custom software is now nearly free. Five years ago, a quick utility took a weekend. Now it's an evening. The Karpathy posture is to do this constantly — board prep quizzers, lab pattern scanners, notes templates. The cost of building falls; the bar for "is this worth making" should fall with it.

tip 04 Keep one hand on the wheel oversight

Vibe coding works for weekend projects; it's dangerous for systems you'll maintain. Even Karpathy hand-codes his serious work. In medicine this is doubly true: a draft note from an ambient scribe is a draft. Your signature still means what it always meant.

My AI stack

A best-of-class survey of AI tools, picked for a clinician who builds — Chalk Talk, Ivrit Journey, and the rest of the working life around them.

About this list. Objective on category-by-category quality (best-in-class for image, coding, vibe-coding, etc.) — and opinionated on which of those fits you specifically. Chat & reasoning

general-purpose

Where most people meet AI. The frontier models leapfrog each other every few months; in April 2026, all three of the below are genuinely excellent and have meaningfully different personalities.

Claude
Anthropic
freemium $20/mo Pro long-form
claude.ai →
A frontier LLM with a strong reputation for nuanced reasoning, careful writing, and following long instructions faithfully — including custom instruction sets like the one running this very book. Excellent at clinical reasoning and at not over-confidently making things up.
Why this is your daily driver: the pedagogical voice you've already calibrated this whole project around, plus Projects (custom instructions + knowledge bases) which is exactly what Working Knowledge runs on. For Chalk Talk: Claude's careful instruction-following is the right baseline for a guideline-grounded clinical tool.
ChatGPT
OpenAI
freemium $20/mo Plus multimodal
chatgpt.com →
The default AI for most of the world, and rightly so. Strong on coding and math, fastest at multimodal (voice, vision, image gen all in one chat), and the GPT-5 reasoning models are top-tier on hard logic.
When to reach for it: when you need image generation, voice mode, and chat in one app, or when you want a second opinion on something Claude said. The Voice mode specifically is the best in the field for hands-free conversation.
Gemini
Google DeepMind
freemium $20/mo Pro long context
gemini.google.com →
Gemini 3 is genuinely competitive at the frontier, with a unique advantage in very long context windows and tight integration with Google products (Gmail, Docs, Calendar). The image generation (Nano Banana Pro) is currently best-in-class.
When to reach for it: when you need to feed in a very long document (100+ pages), or when you live in Gmail and want AI that actually knows your schedule and inbox.
Verdict: Run Claude as your primary, ChatGPT as your second opinion + voice, Gemini when you need long context or Google integration. All three free tiers are usable; pay for whichever one becomes your daily.

Coding (serious)

for Chalk Talk, Ivrit Journey

Tools where you're maintaining a codebase you care about. Different from vibe-coding, where you're building something throwaway. As of April 2026, the field is consolidating around these three.

Claude Code
Anthropic
$20+/mo terminal agentic
claude.ai/code →
Terminal-native AI coding agent, currently the highest-ranked tool by serious developers (46% "most loved" in the Pragmatic Engineer's Feb 2026 survey, ~4% of all public GitHub commits). Excels at multi-file reasoning and large refactors. Up to 1M token context on Max tier.
Why this is the pick: for Chalk Talk and Ivrit Journey, you need a tool that can hold the whole codebase in its head and iterate carefully. Claude Code's plan→edit→test→fix loop is the best in class for this. Lives in your terminal so it doesn't replace your editor — you stay in control.
Cursor
Anysphere
$20+/mo IDE multi-model
cursor.com →
VS Code fork with AI woven into every keystroke. Fast Tab autocomplete, multi-file Composer mode, multi-model routing (Claude, GPT-5, Gemini, their own Composer). The best AI-native IDE, period.
When to reach for it: if you prefer editor-first to terminal-first, or want to switch between models within a session. Many devs run both — Cursor for editing, Claude Code for autonomous tasks.
Codex (CLI)
OpenAI
paid cloud sandbox async
openai.com/codex →
OpenAI's coding agent, recently passed 3M weekly users. Distinguishing feature: cloud sandbox model designed for asynchronous, long-running tasks that proceed without you watching. Now ships as a plugin inside Claude Code.
When to reach for it: when you want to fire off a multi-hour refactor and check on it later. Less compelling as your daily driver than Claude Code or Cursor.
Verdict: Pick Claude Code as primary; add Cursor if you want IDE-style editing alongside. The whole field is converging — by next year these will all interoperate via plugins, and you'll mix and match.

Vibe coding

prompt → working app

Tools designed for the Karpathy throwaway-weekend-project use case: describe what you want, get a working app, ship it without thinking too hard about the code. Very different from "serious coding" — the bar for production-readiness is lower; the bar for speed-of-iteration is higher.

Lovable
Lovable AB
freemium $20+/mo full-stack
lovable.dev →
Type a description of the app you want, get a working full-stack app with frontend, backend, and database. The most polished of the prompt-to-app tools as of early 2026. Outputs are real code you can keep iterating on.
Why this is the pick for you: you already have the builder instinct (Chalk Talk, Ivrit Journey didn't appear out of thin air). Lovable shortens the gap from idea to working prototype from a weekend to an hour. Use it for: the next residency dashboard or board-prep quizzer that crosses your mind. Build it before the impulse fades.
Bolt
StackBlitz
freemium browser live preview
bolt.new →
Same general flavor as Lovable — prompt-to-app — but runs in-browser with instant live preview. Fast feedback loop, especially good for visual UI work.
When to reach for it: when you want to see something on screen in 30 seconds. Slightly less "real codebase" energy than Lovable, but faster to start.
v0
Vercel
freemium UI-focused
v0.dev →
Generates React + Tailwind UI components from prompts. Narrower than Lovable/Bolt, but exceptional at the specific job of "make this UI look professional."
When to reach for it: when you have a working app but its UI looks like a 1998 form. Hand a screenshot to v0 and ask for a redesign.
Replit Agent
Replit
freemium deploys
replit.com →
Browser IDE with an agent that can build full apps and deploy them. Strong "ship it now" energy. (Note: had a high-profile incident in 2025 where the agent deleted a production database despite being told not to — caveat the autonomy.)
Verdict: Lovable for new ideas, v0 for cleaning up UIs. The Karpathy oversight rule applies — vibe-code prototypes happily, but anything you'll maintain or rely on (Chalk Talk, Ivrit Journey) belongs in Claude Code or Cursor where you actually read the diffs.

Image generation

creative & medical illustration

The image-gen race has moved fast. As of April 2026, the leader is Google's Nano Banana Pro (yes, that's the actual name) on Gemini 3, with Midjourney still the choice for artistic/cinematic, and ChatGPT's GPT-image best-integrated for casual use.

Nano Banana Pro 🍌
Google · Gemini 3 Pro Image
freemium SOTA text-in-image
via Gemini →
Google's flagship image model, built on Gemini 3. State-of-the-art on photorealism, infographics, and the historically hard problem of legible text inside images. Up to 4K, multi-language text rendering, search-grounded for factual visuals.
Why this is the pick: you're a clinician-educator. The killer use case is generating infographics, diagrams, and teaching slides with accurate labels — kidney anatomy with correctly spelled tubule names, drug-mechanism diagrams that don't hallucinate the molecule. Older models butchered text-in-image; this one doesn't.
GPT image generation
OpenAI · inside ChatGPT
freemium conversational
chatgpt.com →
ChatGPT's native image generation. Excellent at conversational iteration ("now make her wearing a stethoscope") because it shares context with the chat. Slightly behind Nano Banana on text-in-image but ahead on style consistency across edits.
When to reach for it: when you're already chatting and just need an image. Lowest friction.
Midjourney
Midjourney Inc
$10+/mo artistic
midjourney.com →
Still the gold standard for cinematic, painterly, "wow that's beautiful" images. Less practical for diagrams or anything requiring text/realism, more about aesthetic excellence.
When to reach for it: book-cover-quality hero images, mood boards, conceptual art for a talk's title slide.
Verdict: Nano Banana Pro for medical illustrations and slide graphics; ChatGPT image gen for casual one-offs. Skip Midjourney unless you're going for a specific aesthetic — for clinical work it's overkill.

Voice & dictation

talk to AI faster

Karpathy's tip 01 in flesh: stop typing, start talking. Voice-to-text into AI is one of the highest-ROI workflow changes available right now.

Wispr Flow
Wispr
freemium $15/mo Pro macOS/iOS
wisprflow.ai →
System-wide voice dictation that works in any text field — Slack, Claude, your EHR, anywhere. Holds for hold-to-talk, releases when you let go. Auto-formats and auto-punctuates. The current top choice for "I want to talk to my computer all day."
Why this is the pick: 4-5x faster than typing for the kind of long, discursive prompts that get good answers from Claude. Clinical use: dictate patient summaries between consults, then paste into AI for organization. Pairs beautifully with the Karpathy "talk to it" tip.
SuperWhisper
Superwhisper
freemium macOS local mode
superwhisper.com →
The tool Karpathy actually uses. Similar to Wispr Flow but with strong local/offline mode (transcription happens on your machine, not in the cloud) — meaningful for clinical dictation where privacy matters.
When to reach for it: if you want offline transcription, or if you find Wispr Flow's UX not quite right.
Verdict: Try Wispr Flow first; the polish edge is real. Switch to SuperWhisper if local-only matters. Either way, install one this week — the workflow change is real.

Research & search

finding things, with citations

When you want answers grounded in real, current sources — the way Google should have evolved.

Perplexity
Perplexity AI
freemium $20/mo Pro cited
perplexity.ai →
AI-native search engine. Every answer comes with numbered citations to specific sources. The Pro tier adds deep research mode (multi-step searches that take a few minutes and produce report-quality output).
Why this is the pick: you have biostats-grade skepticism. Perplexity's citations let you verify before you trust. For clinical use: "what is the current evidence for SGLT2 inhibitors in non-diabetic CKD" returns a synthesis with the actual papers cited, not vibes.
OpenEvidence
OpenEvidence
free for clinicians medical only
openevidence.com →
Clinical-AI-specific search tool. Trained on and grounded in peer-reviewed medical literature only. Free for verified clinicians. Not a substitute for UpToDate but increasingly a first stop for "what does the latest evidence say."
When to reach for it: point-of-care clinical questions, especially when UpToDate feels slow or out-of-date on something fast-moving (e.g., new GLP-1 indications).
Claude / ChatGPT Research mode
Anthropic / OpenAI
paid tier deep research
Both have multi-step research modes that take 5-15 minutes and produce comprehensive reports with citations. The serious cousin of one-shot search. Useful for "give me the lay of the land on X" before reading any actual papers.
Verdict: Perplexity as your default search-replacement; OpenEvidence for clinical-only questions; Claude/ChatGPT research mode when you'd otherwise spend an afternoon on PubMed.

Notes, podcasts, knowledge

turning information into formats you'll consume

The category that didn't exist three years ago — taking a stack of documents and turning them into something you'll actually engage with.

NotebookLM
Google
free audio overview RAG built-in
notebooklm.google.com →
Upload sources (papers, PDFs, web pages, YouTube videos), and NotebookLM gives you a chat grounded only in those sources, plus an "audio overview" — a two-host podcast-style discussion of your sources, generated in 5 minutes.
Why this is the pick: your custom instructions specifically request NotebookLM-able sources. The audio overview turns Working Knowledge chapters or stacks of nephrology papers into 15-minute podcasts you can listen to on a walk or while Winston naps. Genuinely changes the relationship between you and the medical literature.
Granola
Granola
freemium macOS meeting notes
granola.ai →
AI meeting notes that listen in the background and give you formatted, actionable notes after the call. The current favorite among PMs and researchers; quietly excellent.
When to reach for it: any meeting where you'd otherwise scribble. Especially good for QI meetings, project syncs, the SF interview circuit you'll be doing.
Obsidian + Smart Connections
community
free local power-user
obsidian.md →
Local-first markdown note-taking. With the Smart Connections plugin, you get embedding-based "find related notes" inside your own vault. Real RAG over your own knowledge base.
When to reach for it: if you want a serious second brain. Steeper learning curve than Notion; more rewarding once you've climbed it. For Chalk Talk: good place to draft the conceptual scaffolding before it becomes code.
Verdict: NotebookLM is non-negotiable for this curriculum. Add Granola the next time you start a project where meetings matter.

Embeddings: meaning as geometry

The single most clarifying idea in modern AI.

Already complete ✓

You completed this chapter. The key takeaway: an embedding is a learned vector that represents meaning as a position in space. Similar things end up near each other; arithmetic on meanings becomes possible (king − man + woman ≈ queen). This is the substrate under semantic search, RAG, and every LLM's internal representations.
